Privity of Contract and Consideration: The Scope of Third-Party Rights

October 2024

I. Introduction

The common law doctrine of privity of contract, which provides that only parties to a contract may sue or be sued upon it, has been received into Indian jurisprudence with certain significant modifications. Section 2(d) of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, defines consideration in terms that have been interpreted by the courts to permit, in limited circumstances, a relaxation of the strict privity rule. This divergence from the English common law position reflects a distinctive approach to contractual obligations, one that balances doctrinal purity with the demands of commercial practice and equitable considerations.

The tension between the general rule of privity and the statutory definition of consideration has generated considerable judicial elaboration over more than a century. While Indian courts have consistently affirmed that a stranger to a contract cannot, as a general proposition, enforce its terms, they have simultaneously recognised a series of exceptions that permit third-party enforcement in specified circumstances. This article examines the statutory foundation of the privity rule in Indian law, analyses the recognised exceptions and their doctrinal basis, and considers whether the current position achieves an appropriate balance between contractual certainty and equitable flexibility.

II. The Statutory Framework

The Indian Contract Act, 1872, departs from the English common law in its definition of consideration. Section 2(d) provides:

"When, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing, something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise."

The phrase “any other person” is of critical importance. It codifies the principle that consideration may move from a third party, and not necessarily from the promisee. This statutory language explicitly rejects the English rule that consideration must move from the promisee, a position affirmed in Tweddle v. Atkinson (1861). The Indian legislature, by this drafting choice, created the doctrinal space for a more flexible approach to third-party rights.

The definition in Section 2(d) must be read together with Section 2(a), which defines a proposal, and Section 2(b), which defines a promise. Together, these provisions establish that a contract arises from an offer and its acceptance, supported by consideration moving from the promisee or any other person. The statute thus separates the requirement of consideration from the requirement of privity, a distinction that has proven to be of considerable practical significance.

Notwithstanding this expansive definition of consideration, Section 2(d) does not expressly address the question of who may enforce a contract. It speaks to the sufficiency of consideration, not to the standing of third parties to sue. This silence has necessitated judicial elaboration of the relationship between consideration and privity.

III. The Privity Rule

Indian courts have consistently affirmed the general rule of privity despite the broader definition of consideration. A person who is not a party to a contract cannot, as a general proposition, enforce its terms.

The leading authority remains the Privy Council decision in Jamna Das v. Ram Autar (1911). The case involved a contract for the sale of property where the purchaser agreed to pay a sum of money to a third party. When the purchaser failed to pay, the third party sought to enforce the agreement. The Privy Council held that while consideration may move from a third party, that third party cannot sue on the contract unless they are also a party to it.

Lord Macnaghten observed that under the Indian Contract Act, consideration may move from a third party, but that does not destroy the fundamental principle that a stranger to a contract cannot sue.

The Supreme Court reaffirmed this position in M.C. Chacko v. State Bank of Travancore (1969). The Court held that a stranger to a contract cannot sue, while acknowledging the recognised exceptions.

In Krishna Lal Gupta v. Dharam Pal (1975), the Delhi High Court emphasised that the privity rule serves important functions: it protects parties from liability to persons they did not intend to benefit, prevents multiplicity of litigation, and respects contractual autonomy.

IV. Exceptions to the Privity Rule

1. Trusts and Charges: The first and most well-established exception concerns trusts and charges. Where a contract creates a trust or a charge in favour of a third party, that third party may enforce the contract. In Khwaja Muhammad Khan v. Husaini Begum (1910), the Privy Council held that where property is charged with the payment of a sum of money to a third party, that third party may sue to enforce the charge even though not a party to the contract creating it. The Supreme Court applied this principle in Narayani Devi v. Tagore Commercial Corporation Ltd. (1976).

2.Family Settlements and Marriage Arrangements: In Shuppu Ammal v. Subramaniyan (1910), the Madras High Court held that where a contract is entered into for the benefit of a family member in the context of a family arrangement, that family member may sue to enforce it. The Supreme Court affirmed this approach in Raja Rai Bhagwat Dayal Singh v. Ram Ratan Singh (1921).

3. Contracts of Agency: Section 230 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that in the absence of contrary intention, an agent contracts on behalf of the principal, and the principal may sue and be sued on the contract.

4. Covenants Running with the Land: In Jugraj Singh v. Labh Singh (1995), the Supreme Court held that a purchaser of property with notice of a prior agreement affecting the property may be bound by that agreement even though not a party to it.

5. Statutory Exceptions: Various statutes create specific exceptions to the privity rule. The Insurance Act, 1938 permits beneficiaries of life insurance policies to enforce policies directly, while the Companies Act, 2013 creates enforceable rights arising from company constitutions.

V. Analysis: Principle and Policy

The Indian law on privity and consideration represents a distinctive synthesis of common law principles and statutory modification.

In Deb Narayan Dutt v. Chunilal Ghose (1914), the Calcutta High Court observed that where a contract is made for the benefit of a third party and that party acts on the faith of it to their detriment, an equity arises in their favour that the courts will protect. However, courts have consistently resisted arguments for a generalised exception permitting third-party enforcement wherever a contract purports to confer a benefit on a third party.

In Krishna Bhat v. Shri Ganesh (2020), the Karnataka High Court emphasised that the recognised exceptions must be applied cautiously and do not represent a general departure from the privity rule.

VI. Implications for Commercial Contracting

Parties cannot assume that intended third-party beneficiaries will automatically possess enforceable rights. If enforcement rights are intended, the third party should ideally be joined as a contracting party.

Trust structures remain a useful mechanism for conferring enforceable rights on third parties, while agency arrangements must be clearly documented.

Where property is involved, covenants and charges should be properly recorded to bind subsequent purchasers.

VII. Conclusion

The Indian law on privity of contract and consideration represents a considered adaptation of English common law principles to local statutory conditions. The recognised exceptions—trusts and charges, family settlements, agency relationships, covenants running with land, and statutory provisions—form a coherent framework for third-party rights.

The resulting law is neither a slavish imitation of English precedent nor a radical departure from common law principles. It represents a distinctive synthesis that attempts to balance contractual certainty with equitable flexibility.
