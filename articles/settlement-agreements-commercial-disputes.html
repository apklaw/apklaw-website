Settlement Agreements in Commercial Disputes

February 2026

I. Introduction

Settlement agreements are a common feature of commercial disputes. Parties who find themselves in disagreement over contractual performance often negotiate a compromise, record its terms in a settlement agreement, and receive payment in full and final settlement of all claims. The expectation is that this brings finality to the dispute. The matter is closed. The parties move on.

Yet the legal finality of such settlements is not always absolute. Courts are sometimes called upon to determine whether a settlement agreement truly resolves all disputes or whether it can be reopened. The concept of accord and satisfaction embodied in Section 63 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, provides the legal framework for analysing these questions. When a party alleges that a full and final settlement was procured by fraud, coercion, or undue influence, or when a dispute arises about whether there was any settlement at all, the courts must decide whether the matter can be reopened.

This article examines the legal principles governing the finality of settlement agreements in commercial disputes. It analyses the doctrine of accord and satisfaction under the Indian Contract Act, considers the circumstances in which courts permit reopening of settled claims, and examines the procedural question of whether disputes about the validity of a settlement should be decided by a court or referred to arbitration.

II. The Statutory Framework: Accord and Satisfaction under Section 63

The concept of accord and satisfaction is embodied in Section 63 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The section provides that every promisee may dispense with or remit, wholly or in part, the performance of the promise made to him, or may extend the time for such performance, or may accept instead of it any satisfaction which he thinks fit.

A contract between parties can come to an end by performance, where all obligations are fulfilled in terms of the original contract. This is referred to as discharge by performance. Alternatively, the contract may also be discharged by substitution of certain new obligations in place of the obligations contained in the original contract, and subsequent performance of the substituted obligations. The substituted obligations are referred to as accord and the discharge of the substituted obligations is referred to as satisfaction. In common parlance, this is known as discharge by accord and satisfaction or by full and final settlement.

There must be a meeting of minds between the parties that the new arrangement replaces the old obligations. If a party simply makes a payment and unilaterally declares it to be in full settlement, without the other party's agreement to that effect, there is no accord and satisfaction. The question of whether the parties have in fact agreed to a settlement is a mixed question of law and fact, to be determined on the evidence.

III. The Krish Spinning Framework

The Supreme Court's decision in SBI General Insurance Co. Ltd. v. Krish Spinning (2024) provides the most comprehensive recent analysis of accord and satisfaction in the context of arbitration. The case arose from a dispute under an insurance policy. After negotiations, the insurer issued a cheque for a sum in full and final settlement, which the insured encashed. Subsequently, the insured invoked arbitration claiming additional amounts. The question was whether the encashment of the cheque barred the arbitration.

The Supreme Court held that once a contract has been fully performed, it can be said to have been discharged by performance. Once the contract has been discharged by performance, neither any right to seek performance, nor any obligation to perform remains under it. However, whether there has been a discharge of contract or not is a mixed question of law and fact. If any dispute arises as to whether a contract has been discharged or not, such a dispute is arbitrable as per the mechanism prescribed under the arbitration agreement contained in the underlying contract.

The Court clarified that even if the contracting parties, in pursuance of a settlement, agree to discharge each other of any obligations arising under the contract, this does not ipso facto mean that the arbitration agreement would also come to an end, unless the parties expressly agree to do the same. The intention of the parties in discharging a contract by accord and satisfaction is to relieve each other of the existing or any new obligations under the contract. Such a discharge of obligations under the substantive contract cannot be construed to mean that the parties also intended to relieve each other of their obligation to settle any dispute pertaining to the original contract through arbitration.

The Court further held that although ordinarily no arbitrable disputes may subsist after execution of a full and final settlement, yet any dispute pertaining to the full and final settlement itself, by necessary implication being a dispute arising out of or in relation to or under the substantive contract, would not be precluded from reference to arbitration. The arbitration agreement contained in the original contract continues to be in existence even after the parties have discharged the original contract by accord and satisfaction.

IV. Distinguishing Accord and Satisfaction from Novation

It is useful to distinguish accord and satisfaction from the related concept of novation under Section 62 of the Contract Act. Novation occurs when the parties to a contract agree to substitute a new contract for the original one. The original contract need not be performed because it has been replaced.

The distinction matters for the survival of ancillary rights such as arbitration clauses. In B.L. Kashyap and Sons Limited v. Mist Avenue Private Limited (2023), the Delhi High Court examined whether an arbitration clause survives when parties enter into a new contract that novates the original agreement. The Court held that where the new contract constitutes a wholesale novation of the original contract, the arbitration clause would also stand extinguished by virtue of the new agreement.

In that case, the parties had entered into a construction contract containing an arbitration clause. Disputes arose and were settled through a Memorandum of Understanding that recorded the settlement terms and expressly cancelled the original construction contract. The MOU did not contain an arbitration clause. When disputes arose under the MOU, one party sought to invoke the arbitration clause from the original contract. The Court held that the original contract was extinguished by the substituted MOU, and the arbitration clause perished with it.

V. Conditional Payment and the Question of Agreement

A recurring issue in settlement disputes concerns conditional payments. A debtor sends a cheque for a sum less than the amount claimed, stating that it is tendered in full and final settlement. The creditor encashes the cheque but immediately protests that it is not accepted in full settlement. Does this constitute accord and satisfaction?

This question was addressed by the Allahabad High Court in Basdeo Ram Sarup v. Dilsukhrai Sewak Ram (1922). The Court held that if the plaintiffs had agreed to accept a smaller sum in full payment, it would not be open to them to go behind that admission. But if they did not agree, Section 63 would have no application. The main question was whether the plaintiffs did in fact agree to accept the money in full satisfaction.

The Court referred to the English decision in Day v. McLea (1889). It was held that if a person sends money on terms that it is to be taken, if at all, in satisfaction of a larger claim, and the money is kept, it is a question of fact as to the terms upon which it is so kept. Accord and satisfaction imply an agreement to take the money in satisfaction. There must be either two minds agreeing, or one party acting in such a way as to induce the other to think that the money is taken in satisfaction.

VI. Grounds for Reopening Settled Claims

Even where a settlement agreement exists and has been performed, courts may permit reopening in limited circumstances. The Supreme Court in Krish Spinning reiterated that there is no rule of an absolute kind which precludes arbitration in cases where a full and final settlement has been arrived at.

The Court referred to National Insurance Co. Ltd. v. Boghara Polyfab Pvt. Ltd. and R.L. Kalathia and Company v. State of Gujarat, reiterating that the mere execution of a discharge voucher or no dues certificate would not by itself operate as a bar to arbitration where the validity of such documents is challenged on grounds of fraud, coercion, or undue influence.

VII. Who Decides the Validity of a Settlement?

The Supreme Court in SBI General Insurance Co. Ltd. v. Krish Spinning traced the evolution of the law on this question and reaffirmed that where an arbitration agreement exists, disputes regarding whether a settlement has discharged the contract are generally for the arbitrator to decide.

The Court also referred to Duro Felguera, S.A. v. Gangavaram Port Ltd., which held that the scope of enquiry under Section 11 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 is confined to determining the existence of an arbitration agreement.

VIII. Conclusion

Settlement agreements serve the important purpose of bringing finality to commercial disputes. Parties who negotiate a compromise and receive payment in full settlement are entitled to expect that the matter is concluded.

Yet finality is not absolute. Courts have recognised that settlement agreements may be reopened in limited circumstances, particularly where consent is vitiated by fraud, coercion, or undue influence, or where there was no genuine agreement to the settlement terms.

The survival of arbitration clauses in the context of settlement is now well settled. An arbitration agreement survives the discharge of the substantive contract by accord and satisfaction unless the parties expressly agree to extinguish it. Any dispute pertaining to the validity or effect of the settlement itself remains arbitrable.

For commercial parties, the lesson is clear. Settlement agreements should be carefully documented with clear language recording the parties' intention to resolve all disputes finally. Where arbitration is intended to survive, this should be stated expressly. These precautions strengthen the enforceability of settlements and reduce the risk of subsequent disputes.
