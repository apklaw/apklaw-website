Impossibility and Suspension of Performance under the Indian Contract Act

December 2024

I. Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic precipitated a wave of contractual disputes that forced Indian courts to revisit the boundaries of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The doctrine of frustration, long confined to situations of physical or legal impossibility, faced renewed scrutiny as parties sought to invoke force majeure clauses or the frustration doctrine to excuse performance rendered economically burdensome by the pandemic. Lockdowns, supply chain disruptions, and labour shortages transformed once-profitable contracts into loss-making obligations, prompting litigants to ask a fundamental question: Does severe economic hardship constitute frustration under Indian law?

This article examines whether Indian courts have expanded the doctrine of "impossibility" to include economic hardship. It analyses recent High Court rulings on long-term supply agreements, energy purchase contracts, and commercial lease arrangements, and considers whether the pandemic has effected a permanent shift in the judicial approach to contractual frustration. The article argues that while courts have demonstrated flexibility in applying Section 56, they have consistently resisted attempts to transform the doctrine into a general relief mechanism for bad bargains. The doctrinal tension between strict contractual enforcement and equitable relief remains unresolved, with significant implications for commercial contracting practice.

II. The Statutory Framework

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, embodies the doctrine of frustration in Indian law. It provides:

"An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful."

The provision encompasses two distinct situations: initial impossibility under the first paragraph, and supervening impossibility or illegality under the second paragraph. The second paragraph is the foundation of the frustration doctrine in India.

The leading authoritative interpretation remains the Supreme Court's decision in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954). The Court held that the doctrine of frustration applies not only to physical impossibility but also to situations where the fundamental basis of the contract has been destroyed, rendering performance "impossible" in a commercial sense. Chief Justice Das observed:

"The word 'impossible' has not been used in the section in the sense of physical or literal impossibility. The performance of an act may not be literally impossible but it may be impracticable and useless from the point of view of the object and purpose which the parties had in view."

This formulation appeared to open the door to frustration claims based on commercial impracticability. However, subsequent decisions significantly narrowed this interpretation, requiring that the supervening event fundamentally alter the nature of the contractual obligation rather than merely making performance more difficult or expensive.

III. The Energy Watchdog Standard

The Supreme Court in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (2017) established the governing standard for frustration claims under Section 56. The case arose from changes in Indonesian coal pricing regulations that made coal-fired power projects economically unviable. The Court rejected the frustration claim, holding that the performance of a contract is not discharged merely because it has become onerous for a party due to unforeseen events.

Justice Nariman observed that the supervening event should take away the very foundation of the agreement. A party cannot rely on a force majeure event if the contract becomes onerous on account of the event. The increase in price, even if substantial, would not be enough to discharge the contract. This formulation—the "total destruction" test—established a high threshold for frustration claims. Commercial impracticability, however severe, did not suffice. The contract must have become "radically different" from that which the parties contemplated.

The Court emphasised that it is imperative to prove that performance has become objectively impossible, not merely financially detrimental. This distinction between impossibility and mere onerousness has proved decisive in subsequent cases. The Energy Watchdog standard reflects a policy choice in favour of contractual certainty over equitable relief for parties who have made bad bargains.

IV. The Question of Temporary Impossibility

Neither Section 56 nor the Energy Watchdog decision directly addresses the treatment of temporary impossibility. Where performance becomes temporarily impossible due to lockdowns, strikes, or natural disasters, does the contract become permanently void under Section 56, or is performance suspended until the impossibility ceases?

The distinction is significant. If temporary impossibility results in permanent discharge, parties may be freed from contracts that could still be performed once the impediment is removed. This could produce windfalls for parties seeking to escape contracts that have become merely inconvenient rather than impossible. If, on the other hand, performance is merely suspended, parties remain bound and must perform once the impediment is removed, even if the delay has made performance less valuable.

The statutory language of Section 56 does not address this question directly. It provides that a contract becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful. It does not distinguish between permanent and temporary impossibility. The question has therefore been left to judicial development.

V. Pandemic-Era Decisions on Temporary Impossibility

The COVID-19 pandemic generated a body of case law addressing temporary impossibility directly. Several decisions provide guidance on how Indian courts approach this question.

In Standard Retail Pvt. Ltd. v. G.S. Global Corp. (2020), the Bombay High Court considered a force majeure clause in a commodities supply contract. The court granted interim relief to a party seeking to invoke the clause, accepting that the pandemic and resulting lockdown constituted a force majeure event. However, the court emphasised that the inquiry remained contractual: the parties' allocation of risk, as expressed in their agreement, governed. The court contemplated that performance would resume when restrictions lifted, treating the impossibility as temporary and the contract as suspended rather than discharged.

In MEP Infrastructure Developers Ltd. v. South Delhi Municipal Corporation (2020), the Delhi High Court considered whether a toll collection contract could be frustrated by pandemic-related traffic restrictions. The contractor sought extension of the concession period, arguing that lockdowns had rendered performance impossible. The court did not invoke Section 56 to declare the contract void. Instead, it directed the municipal corporation to consider the representation sympathetically.

More significantly, in Ramanand & Ors. v. Dr. N.R. & Ors. (2020), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim by tenants seeking rent relief during the lockdown. The court held that the pandemic did not render the lease agreements void under Section 56, as the obligation to pay rent remained capable of performance.

A notable divergence emerged in Halliburton Offshore Services Inc. v. Vedanta Limited (2020), where the Delhi High Court granted an interim injunction restraining the invocation of a bank guarantee.

In Indian Performing Right Society Ltd. v. Entertainment Network (India) Ltd. (2021), the Bombay High Court rejected a frustration claim based on pandemic-related cancellation of live music events.

VIII. Conclusion

The COVID-19 pandemic tested but did not fundamentally transform the Indian law of frustration. Courts have demonstrated flexibility in applying Section 56 to unprecedented circumstances but have consistently resisted expanding the doctrine to encompass economic hardship.

On the specific question of temporary impossibility, Indian courts have shown a consistent preference for suspension over permanent discharge.

Parties seeking protection against temporary impossibility must address it expressly in their agreements.
