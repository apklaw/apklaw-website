Impossibility and Suspension of Performance under the Indian Contract Act

February 2026

I. Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic precipitated a wave of contractual disputes that forced Indian courts to revisit the boundaries of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The doctrine of frustration, long confined to situations of physical or legal impossibility, faced renewed scrutiny as parties sought to invoke force majeure clauses or the frustration doctrine to excuse performance rendered economically burdensome by the pandemic. Lockdowns, supply chain disruptions, and labour shortages transformed once-profitable contracts into loss-making obligations, prompting litigants to ask a fundamental question: does severe economic hardship constitute frustration under Indian law?

This article examines whether Indian courts have expanded the doctrine of “impossibility” to include economic hardship. It analyses recent High Court rulings on long-term supply agreements, energy purchase contracts, and commercial lease arrangements, and considers whether the pandemic has effected a permanent shift in the judicial approach to contractual frustration. The article argues that while courts have demonstrated flexibility in applying Section 56, they have consistently resisted attempts to transform the doctrine into a general relief mechanism for bad bargains.

II. The Statutory Framework: Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 embodies the doctrine of frustration in Indian law. It provides:

“An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful.”

The leading interpretation remains the Supreme Court decision in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954). The Court held that the doctrine applies not only to literal impossibility but also to situations where the fundamental basis of the contract has been destroyed.

Chief Justice Das observed that the term “impossible” does not refer only to physical impossibility. Performance may become impracticable and pointless in light of the purpose which the parties had in view.

III. The Pre-Pandemic Position: A Narrow Doctrine

Before the pandemic, Indian courts adopted a consistently narrow approach to frustration. The Supreme Court in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (2017) reaffirmed that Section 56 applies only where the contractual foundation has been “totally destroyed” and not merely where performance has become more onerous.

Similarly, in Rajasthan Rajya Vidyut Prasaran Nigam Ltd. v. DCM Shriram Consolidated Ltd. (2018), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim based on a dramatic increase in coal prices, emphasising that price escalation is an inherent risk of long-term commercial contracts.

IV. Pandemic-Era Litigation: Testing the Boundaries

Where contracts contained force majeure clauses, courts generally enforced them according to their terms. In Standard Retail Pvt. Ltd. v. G.S. Global Corp. (2020), the Bombay High Court accepted that the pandemic and resulting lockdown constituted a force majeure event but emphasised that the analysis remained contractual in nature.

Where contracts lacked such clauses, parties invoked Section 56 directly. In MEP Infrastructure Developers Ltd. v. South Delhi Municipal Corporation (2020), the Delhi High Court considered whether pandemic-related traffic restrictions frustrated a toll concession contract. The court refrained from declaring the contract void and instead directed the authority to consider relief, suggesting that temporary disruption does not justify permanent discharge.

In Ramanand & Ors. v. Dr. N.R. & Ors. (2020), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim by tenants seeking rent relief during lockdowns, holding that economic hardship does not render contractual obligations impossible.

A divergent approach appeared in Halliburton Offshore Services Inc. v. Vedanta Ltd. (2020), where the Delhi High Court granted interim relief restraining invocation of a bank guarantee, accepting that lockdown restrictions temporarily prevented performance. However, the interim nature of the order limited its precedential value.

V. Doctrinal Analysis: Has the Pandemic Expanded Section 56?

Three years of pandemic litigation suggest that the Energy Watchdog “total destruction” test remains the governing standard. Courts continue to require that the supervening event fundamentally alter the nature of the contractual obligation rather than merely its economics.

Indian courts have consistently rejected economic hardship as frustration. As the Delhi High Court emphasised in Ramanand, the obligation to pay money rarely becomes impossible in law; difficulty in performance does not amount to impossibility.

Where impossibility is temporary, courts generally favour suspension of performance rather than permanent discharge of the contract.

VI. Comparative Perspectives

Indian law’s restrictive approach broadly aligns with English law. In Canary Wharf (BP4) T1 Ltd. v. European Medicines Agency (2019), the English High Court rejected a frustration claim based on Brexit-related disruption.

Civil law jurisdictions adopt a different approach. French law recognises the doctrine of imprévision under Article 1195 of the Civil Code, permitting judicial revision where circumstances render performance excessively onerous. German law similarly recognises relief where the basis of the transaction has fundamentally altered.

VII. Implications for Commercial Contracting

Pandemic-era jurisprudence underscores the importance of contractual risk allocation. Parties cannot rely on Section 56 to relieve economic hardship; the doctrine remains confined to situations of genuine impossibility.

Force majeure clauses should therefore be carefully drafted to address both temporary disruptions and prolonged impediments. Contracts should also contemplate suspension mechanisms, termination rights, and risk-allocation provisions tailored to large-scale disruptions such as pandemics.

VIII. Conclusion

The pandemic has tested but not fundamentally altered the doctrine of frustration under Indian law. Courts have shown flexibility in addressing unprecedented circumstances but have consistently resisted expanding Section 56 to encompass economic hardship.

The “total destruction” test articulated in Energy Watchdog remains the governing principle. Section 56 continues to operate as a narrow safety mechanism for genuine impossibility rather than a general relief mechanism for difficult commercial circumstances.
