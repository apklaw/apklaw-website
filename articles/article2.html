On the Interpretation of Exclusion Clauses in Commercial Contracts in India

July 2025

I. Introduction

Exclusion clauses, by their very nature, seek to define and limit the legal consequences that would otherwise flow from a breach of contract or from tortious conduct. Their interpretation has long occupied a significant place in the law of contract, and the principles developed by courts across jurisdictions reflect a sustained effort to reconcile the demands of commercial certainty with the imperative of fairness between contracting parties. In India, this area of law is shaped by a blend of codified principles under the Indian Contract Act, 1872, and a robust body of judicial precedent.

This article examines the interpretive framework applied by Indian courts to exclusion clauses in commercial contracts. It analyses the requirements for valid incorporation, the principles of construction including the contra proferentem rule, the treatment of negligence and fundamental breach, and the distinction between clauses that exclude liability and those that define the scope of contractual duty.

II. The Statutory Framework

The Indian Contract Act, 1872 does not contain specific provisions governing exclusion clauses. Unlike the English Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977, which provides statutory controls on the reasonableness of exclusion clauses, the Indian Act leaves the validity and interpretation of such clauses to general contractual principles and judicial development.

Section 23 renders agreements void if their object or consideration is unlawful or opposed to public policy. This provision has occasionally been invoked to challenge exclusion clauses alleged to be unconscionable or contrary to public interest.

Section 28 renders void agreements in restraint of legal proceedings. Properly drafted exclusion clauses that limit substantive liability rather than procedural rights generally do not attract the operation of this provision.

In the absence of statutory controls comparable to the English regime, judicial interpretation remains the primary mechanism for regulating exclusion clauses in India.

III. Incorporation of Exclusion Clauses

The threshold question in any dispute concerning an exclusion clause is whether the clause was validly incorporated into the contract. This depends upon whether reasonable notice of the clause was given to the party sought to be bound.

Where the contract is signed, the rule in L'Estrange v. F. Graucob Ltd. applies: a party who signs a contractual document is bound by its terms whether or not they have read them. Indian courts have applied this principle consistently. In Central Inland Water Transport Corporation Ltd. v. Brojo Nath Ganguly, the Supreme Court recognised the binding force of signed contracts while acknowledging that unconscionable terms may be struck down as opposed to public policy.

Where the contract is not signed, the question is whether reasonable notice of the clause was provided. In Thornton v. Shoe Lane Parking Ltd., the English court observed that the more onerous the clause, the greater the degree of notice required.

IV. The Interpretive Framework

Once incorporation is established, the court must determine whether the clause, properly construed, applies to the breach in question. Courts begin with the ordinary meaning of the contractual language but interpret the clause in the context of the contract as a whole.

In Oil and Natural Gas Corporation Ltd. v. SAW Pipes Ltd., the Supreme Court emphasised that contractual terms, including clauses limiting liability, must ordinarily be given effect according to their plain meaning. However, the court also considers the commercial context and the objectives that the parties sought to achieve.

In Bihar State Electricity Board v. Green Rubber Industries, the Court observed that the meaning of a contractual clause must be derived from the contract as a whole rather than from isolated phrases.

V. The Contra Proferentem Rule

The contra proferentem rule provides that ambiguity in a clause drafted by one party should be resolved against that party. The Supreme Court has affirmed the applicability of this principle to exclusion clauses.

In Bihar State Electricity Board v. Green Rubber Industries, the Court held that ambiguity in an exclusion clause must be construed strictly against the party seeking to rely on it.

However, in commercial contracts between sophisticated parties of equal bargaining power, courts are increasingly reluctant to apply the rule unless genuine ambiguity exists.

In United India Insurance Co. Ltd. v. Pushpalaya Printers, the Supreme Court applied the rule to an insurance policy, resolving ambiguity in favour of the insured.

VI. Exclusion of Negligence and Fundamental Breach

English law traditionally required clear words to exclude liability for negligence. In Canada Steamship Lines Ltd. v. The King, the Privy Council articulated a three-stage test to determine whether a clause excludes liability for negligence.

Indian courts have cited this approach with approval. In Chairman, Life Insurance Corporation v. Rajiv Kumar Bhasker, the Supreme Court held that exclusion clauses in insurance contracts must be strictly construed.

The doctrine of fundamental breach was rejected in English law by the House of Lords in Photo Production Ltd. v. Securicor Transport Ltd.. Indian courts have similarly treated the issue as one of contractual interpretation rather than as an absolute rule.

In Alupro Building Systems Pvt. Ltd. v. Ozone Overseas Pvt. Ltd., the Delhi High Court recognised that broadly worded exclusion clauses may extend even to serious breaches if the contractual language clearly indicates such an intention.

VII. Exclusion Versus Definition

Courts have drawn an important distinction between clauses that exclude liability and clauses that define the scope of the contractual duty itself.

In Scottish Special Housing Association v. Wimpey Construction UK Ltd., the English court distinguished between clauses limiting liability for breach and clauses defining the scope of the obligation.

Indian courts have recognised similar conceptual distinctions. In M.R. Engineers and Contractors Pvt. Ltd. v. Som Datt Builders Ltd., the Supreme Court emphasised the need to understand contractual provisions in terms of their functional role within the agreement.

VIII. Doctrinal Analysis and Conclusion

The interpretation of exclusion clauses in Indian law reflects an attempt to balance the principle of freedom of contract with concerns of fairness and transparency.

Indian courts have developed a coherent interpretive framework based on incorporation requirements, strict construction of ambiguous clauses, and careful attention to the commercial context of the agreement.

The absence of statutory regulation comparable to the English Unfair Contract Terms Act places greater responsibility on the judiciary to regulate exclusion clauses through interpretation. Over time, the courts have demonstrated a consistent willingness to respect the parties' allocation of risk while ensuring that exclusion clauses do not operate unfairly or without adequate notice.

This balance—between contractual certainty and interpretive fairness—remains the defining feature of the Indian approach to exclusion clauses.
