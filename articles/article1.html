Clickwrap, Browsewrap, and Signwrap: The Enforceability of Digital Contracts under the Indian Evidence Act

April 2025

I. Introduction

The proliferation of digital commerce has fundamentally altered how contracts are formed. Where physical signatures and paper documents once dominated, consumers and businesses now routinely enter into agreements through website clicks, browsewrap notices, and electronic signatures. A user purchasing software, subscribing to a streaming service, or accepting terms of use typically manifests assent through a mouse click or by merely continuing to browse a website.

Yet this digital transformation has outpaced the statutory framework governing contract formation and evidence. The Indian Contract Act, 1872 predates digital communication by more than a century. The Indian Evidence Act, 1872 similarly contemplates physical documents and handwritten signatures. While subsequent amendments—particularly the Information Technology Act, 2000—have sought to accommodate electronic transactions, significant questions remain about the enforceability of digital contracts.

This article examines how Indian courts treat clickwrap, browsewrap, and signwrap agreements. It analyses the consent requirement under the Contract Act, the evidentiary framework for electronic records under the Evidence Act, and the authentication standards for electronic signatures under the IT Act.

II. Typologies of Digital Contracts

A. Clickwrap Agreements

Clickwrap agreements require the user to manifest affirmative assent by clicking an “I accept” or “I agree” button. The terms are typically presented before or simultaneously with the click, and the user cannot proceed without accepting. This is the most common form of digital contracting for software licences, online purchases, and account registrations.

Clickwrap most closely resembles traditional contract formation. Courts in common law jurisdictions have consistently enforced clickwrap agreements, treating the click as the functional equivalent of a signature.

B. Browsewrap Agreements

Browsewrap agreements purport to bind users merely by their use of a website. Terms are typically accessible through a hyperlink—often located at the bottom of the page—and the user is not required to manifest affirmative assent.

Browsewrap raises fundamental questions about notice and assent. Courts have been significantly more sceptical of browsewrap agreements, requiring conspicuous notice and evidence that the user actually knew of the terms.

C. Signwrap Agreements

Signwrap agreements combine elements of both models. The user signs or clicks to indicate assent to a document that incorporates external terms by reference. For example, a signature page may state that the user agrees to terms available at a specified website.

D. Shrinkwrap and Webwrap

Shrinkwrap agreements—commonly used in software packaging—purport to bind users upon opening the packaging. Webwrap agreements operate similarly when users submit data through a website while terms are accessible via hyperlink.

III. The Contract Act Framework: Offer, Acceptance, and Consent

Section 2 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 defines a contract as an agreement enforceable by law made with the free consent of parties competent to contract.

A. Communication of Offer

Section 4 provides that communication of an offer is complete when it comes to the knowledge of the person to whom it is made. In the digital context, this raises questions about whether hyperlinks sufficiently communicate contractual terms.

In Trimex International FZE Ltd. v. Vedanta Aluminium Ltd. (2010), the Bombay High Court emphasised that offer and acceptance must be unequivocal and unconditional.

B. Acceptance

Section 7 requires that acceptance be absolute and unqualified. Clickwrap acceptance clearly satisfies this requirement. Browsewrap acceptance—mere use of a website—is more problematic.

In LIC of India v. Consumer Education and Research Centre (1995), the Supreme Court observed that acceptance must be voluntary and intentional.

C. Free Consent

Section 14 defines free consent as consent not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake. Digital contracting raises particular questions where users may not actually read lengthy or hyperlinked terms.

IV. The Information Technology Act Framework

A. Electronic Records

Section 4 of the Information Technology Act, 2000 provides that where the law requires information to be in writing, the requirement is satisfied if the information is available in electronic form. Section 5 similarly validates electronic signatures.

B. Electronic Signatures

Section 3 of the IT Act recognises electronic signatures authenticated through specified technological methods. Whether a simple click constitutes an electronic signature remains an open question, though courts have increasingly accepted clickwrap processes where adequate authentication exists.

C. Admissibility of Electronic Records

Sections 65A and 65B of the Indian Evidence Act govern the admissibility of electronic records. Section 65B requires a certificate authenticating the electronic record.

In Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (2014), the Supreme Court held that Section 65B constitutes a complete code for electronic evidence. Later, in Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020), the Court reaffirmed that certification is mandatory but may be produced later with the court’s permission.

V. Judicial Treatment of Digital Contracts in India

Indian courts have increasingly recognised digital contracts as valid and enforceable.

In P.R. Transport Agency v. Union of India (2005), the Allahabad High Court acknowledged the validity of contracts formed electronically.

In World Wrestling Entertainment Inc. v. Reshma Collection (2014), the Delhi High Court enforced a clickwrap agreement, treating the click as valid manifestation of acceptance.

However, in Google India Pvt. Ltd. v. Visakha Industries (2019), the Andhra Pradesh High Court expressed scepticism regarding browsewrap agreements where users may not have actual notice of the terms.

VI. Doctrinal Challenges

A. The Notice Problem

Browsewrap agreements raise questions about whether users have sufficient notice of contractual terms.

B. The Assent Problem

Section 7 of the Contract Act requires acceptance to be communicated. Passive browsing may not constitute communication of acceptance.

C. The Authentication Problem

Electronic contracts must satisfy authentication requirements under the IT Act and evidentiary requirements under Section 65B of the Evidence Act.

D. The Incorporation Problem

Signwrap agreements raise questions about incorporation by reference where terms are accessible through hyperlinks rather than embedded directly in the agreement.

VII. Comparative Perspectives

In Specht v. Netscape Communications Corp. (2002), the United States Court of Appeals held that browsewrap agreements are enforceable only where users have actual or constructive notice of the terms.

European Union consumer law similarly emphasises informed and affirmative consent before contractual terms become binding.

VIII. Practical Implications for Digital Commerce

Businesses should favour clickwrap over browsewrap. Affirmative manifestation of assent through a clear “I accept” mechanism provides stronger evidence of contract formation.

Electronic records should be preserved with detailed logs, timestamps, and version control of contractual terms to ensure compliance with evidentiary requirements.

IX. Conclusion

Digital contracts are now an indispensable component of modern commerce. Indian law has adapted through the combined operation of the Contract Act, the Information Technology Act, and the Evidence Act. Yet important doctrinal uncertainties remain.

The enforceability of digital agreements ultimately turns on the traditional principles of contract formation—notice, consent, and proof. Clickwrap agreements align closely with these principles, while browsewrap agreements continue to pose doctrinal difficulties.

As digital commerce continues to expand, Indian courts will play an increasingly important role in clarifying the legal standards governing electronic contracting.
