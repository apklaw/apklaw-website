A Note on the Duty of Disclosure in Insurance Contracts in India

March 2024

Insurance contracts in India, following English common law tradition, have long been governed by the overarching principle of uberrimae fidei, or “utmost good faith.” This principle places a significant legal burden on the proposer (the individual seeking insurance) to disclose all material facts that could influence an insurer's decision to accept the risk and determine the premium. This duty forms the bedrock of the insuring contract, which is predicated on the information provided by the proposer.

I. The Content of the Duty in India

The duty of disclosure in Indian insurance law is proactive. The proposer is legally obligated to volunteer all material information during the negotiation of the contract, even if not specifically asked in the proposal form. The legal foundation for this is found in the Indian Contract Act, 1872, and more specifically, the Marine Insurance Act, 1963 (Section 19), which codifies the common law position for marine insurance and is often persuasive for non-marine contracts.

The test for what constitutes a “material fact” is an objective one, established by English case law and consistently upheld by Indian courts. The landmark judgment in General Assurance Society Ltd. v. Chandmull Jain (AIR 1966 SC 1644) affirmed that the duty extends to all facts that would affect the judgment of a prudent insurer. The perspective is not that of the proposer, but that of the insurer in assessing the risk.

The consequences of a breach—whether through non-disclosure (suppressio veri) or misrepresentation (suggestio falsi)—are severe. Under Section 45 of the Insurance Act, 1938, which governs life insurance, an insurer can challenge the policy on grounds of misrepresentation or suppression of material facts. For general insurance, the remedy is governed by the general principles of the Contract Act. A material non-disclosure renders the contract voidable at the insurer’s option, allowing them to avoid the contract ab initio (from the beginning), as if it never existed.

II. Reform and Reconsideration in the Indian Context

The strict application of the disclosure duty has attracted criticism in India, particularly when insurers invoked it to reject genuine claims on technical grounds. The Indian legal and regulatory framework has responded, though in a more piecemeal fashion compared to comprehensive reforms like the UK’s Insurance Act 2015.

The most significant reform came with the 2015 amendment to Section 45 of the Insurance Act, 1938. It introduced a protective shield for policyholders, stipulating that no life insurance policy can be called into question on the ground of misrepresentation after three years from its commencement. Even within this period, the insurer must prove that the proposer made a statement that was “fraudulent” or “material to the contract” and that the policyholder had knowledge of its falsity. This moves away from automatic avoidance towards a more balanced approach.

The Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) has also played a proactive role through the IRDAI (Protection of Policyholders’ Interests) Regulations, 2017. These regulations mandate that insurers explicitly list the duties and responsibilities of a proposer, including the duty of disclosure, and require them to provide clear reasons for claim rejections, ensuring accountability.

Furthermore, a significant body of insurance dispute resolution now occurs before Consumer Disputes Redressal Fora under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019. These forums often apply a more liberal interpretation of the disclosure duty, frequently ruling in favour of the policyholder, especially in cases where the non-disclosure was not material to the cause of loss or was on a question not clearly asked in the proposal form.

III. Concluding Observation

The principle of utmost good faith remains a sound and vital pillar of the Indian insurance market. The integrity of the information provided is a non-negotiable precondition for a stable and fair market, as it allows insurers to underwrite risks accurately.

However, a mature insurance law in India has evolved to balance this need for informational integrity with the necessity of fair outcomes for policyholders. Through the 2015 amendment to Section 45, proactive IRDAI regulations, and the interventionist approach of consumer courts, India has moved towards a more nuanced regime. This ensures that while the duty to disclose remains paramount, the remedial consequences of a breach are proportionate and reasonable, protecting the market from fraud while shielding the honest policyholder from technical forfeiture.
