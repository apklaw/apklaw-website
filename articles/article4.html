Privity of Contract and Consideration

October 2025

The common law doctrine of privity of contract, which provides that only parties to a contract may sue or be sued upon it, has been received into Indian jurisprudence with certain significant modifications. Section 2(d) of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, defines consideration in terms that have been interpreted by the courts to permit, in limited circumstances, a relaxation of the strict privity rule. This divergence from the English common law position reflects a distinctive approach to contractual obligations, one that balances doctrinal purity with the demands of commercial practice and equitable considerations.

I. The Statutory Framework and the Definition of Consideration

The Indian Contract Act, 1872, departs from the English common law in its definition of consideration. Section 2(d) provides:

“When, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing, something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise.”

The phrase “any other person” is of critical importance. It codifies the principle that consideration may move from a third party, and not necessarily from the promisee. This statutory language explicitly rejects the English rule that consideration must move from the promisee, a position affirmed in Tweddle v. Atkinson (1861). The Indian legislature, by this drafting choice, created the doctrinal space for a more flexible approach to third-party rights.

The definition in Section 2(d) must be read together with Section 2(a), which defines a proposal, and Section 2(b), which defines a promise. Together, these provisions establish that a contract arises from an offer and its acceptance, supported by consideration moving from the promisee or any other person. The statute thus separates the requirement of consideration from the requirement of privity, a distinction that has proven to be of considerable practical significance.

II. The Privity Rule and Its Exceptions in Indian Law

Notwithstanding the expansive definition of consideration, Indian courts have consistently affirmed the general rule of privity. A person who is not a party to a contract cannot, as a general proposition, enforce its terms. The leading authority remains the Privy Council decision in Jamna Das v. Ram Autar (1911), which held that consideration may move from a third party, but that third party cannot sue on the contract unless they are also a party to it.

The Supreme Court of India has, over several decades, articulated a series of exceptions to the strict privity rule. These exceptions, grounded in equity and commercial convenience, permit a third party to enforce a contract in specified circumstances.

The first and most well-established exception concerns trusts and charges. Where a contract creates a trust or a charge in favour of a third party, that third party may enforce the contract. In Khwaja Muhammad Khan v. Husaini Begum (1910), the Privy Council held that where property is charged with the payment of a sum of money to a third party, that third party may sue to enforce the charge even though not a party to the contract creating it.

The second exception arises in the context of family settlements and marriage arrangements. In Shuppu Ammal v. Subramaniyan (1910), the Madras High Court held that where a contract is entered into for the benefit of a family member in the context of a family arrangement, that family member may sue to enforce it.

The third and most significant exception in modern commercial practice concerns contracts entered into by an agent on behalf of a disclosed or undisclosed principal. Section 230 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 provides that in the absence of contrary intention, an agent contracts on behalf of the principal, and the principal may sue and be sued on the contract.

A fourth exception has been recognised in cases of covenants running with the land. Where a person purchases land with notice that the owner is bound by a covenant affecting the land, the purchaser may be bound by, and entitled to enforce, the covenant.

III. The Leading Authorities and Their Doctrinal Significance

The modern Indian law on privity and consideration is best understood through a series of Supreme Court decisions that have elaborated and refined the exceptions.

In M.C. Chacko v. State Bank of Travancore (1969), the Supreme Court reaffirmed the general rule that a stranger to a contract cannot sue, while acknowledging the recognised exceptions.

The decision in Deb Narayan Dutt v. Chunilal Ghose (1914), though rendered by the Calcutta High Court, has been influential. The Court held that where a contract is made for the benefit of a third party, and the third party, on the faith of it, acts to their detriment, an equity arises in favour of the third party that the courts will protect.

In Jugraj Singh v. Labh Singh (1995), the Supreme Court considered the intersection of the privity rule with the Transfer of Property Act, 1882. The Court held that a purchaser of property with notice of a prior agreement affecting the property may be bound by that agreement even though not a party to it.

More recently, in Krishna Bhat v. Shri Ganesh (2020), the Karnataka High Court surveyed the authorities and reiterated that while the general rule of privity remains, the recognised exceptions now constitute a coherent body of law. The Court emphasised that the underlying principle is one of intention: where the contract reveals an intention to confer a benefit on a third party, and that third party has relied upon the arrangement, the courts may in appropriate circumstances give effect to that intention.

IV. Closing Observation

The Indian law on privity of contract and consideration represents a distinctive synthesis of English common law principles and statutory modification. By defining consideration to include acts done by “any other person,” the Indian Contract Act created the doctrinal foundation for a more flexible approach than that prevailing in English law.

Over more than a century of judicial development, Indian courts have built upon this foundation a body of doctrine that respects the general rule of privity while acknowledging the demands of equity and commercial practice. The resulting law is neither a slavish imitation of English precedent nor a radical departure from common law principles. It is instead a considered adaptation that seeks to give effect to the intentions of contracting parties while protecting the legitimate expectations of those on whose behalf contracts are made.
