Arbitral Seat and Venue: Persistent Ambiguities in Indian Arbitration Law

October 2025

The distinction between the juridical seat of an arbitration and the physical venue at which hearings are conducted has been a source of considerable doctrinal difficulty in Indian arbitration law. The significance of the distinction lies in its profound consequences for the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts: the courts of the seat possess exclusive curial jurisdiction over the arbitral proceedings, while the courts at the venue, being merely a convenient location for hearings, do not. Despite decades of arbitration jurisprudence, this seemingly elementary distinction continues to generate litigation, reflecting persistent ambiguities in the interpretation of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996.

I. The Seat-Venue Distinction in Principle

The concept of the juridical seat is a creature of private international law and not merely a description of geography. It is the legal home of the arbitration, a construct that may bear no relation to where the arbitrators actually sit. The seat determines the lex arbitri — the law governing the arbitral procedure — and confers jurisdiction on the courts of that jurisdiction to supervise, support, and, where necessary, intervene in the arbitration. A change of physical venue for the convenience of hearings, witnesses, or the parties does not, without more, alter the seat of the arbitration.

This distinction, well established in English arbitration law following cases such as Naviera Amazonica Peruana SA v. Compania Internacional de Seguros del Peru and Channel Tunnel Group Ltd. v. Balfour Beatty Ltd., has been received with less consistency in the Indian context. The Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, in Section 20, employs the term “place of arbitration” without clearly distinguishing between the seat and the venue.

Sub-section (1) provides that the parties are free to agree on the place of arbitration. Sub-section (3) permits the arbitral tribunal to meet at any place it considers appropriate for consultation among its members, for hearing witnesses, experts, or the parties, or for inspection of documents, goods, or other property.

The statutory language invites ambiguity: is the “place” under sub-section (1) the juridical seat, and the locations under sub-section (3) mere venues? The Act does not explicitly say so, and the courts have not always resolved this ambiguity in a manner consistent with the underlying principles of international arbitration. This linguistic imprecision has proven to be fertile ground for litigation.

II. The Supreme Court's Treatment

A series of Supreme Court decisions has sought to clarify the position, with varying degrees of success. The decisions in Bharat Aluminium Co. v. Kaiser Aluminium Technical Services (2012), Union of India v. Hardy Exploration and Production (2018), and Brahmani River Pellets Ltd. v. Kamachi Industries Ltd. (2019) together represent a body of jurisprudence on the question, though they do not speak with complete consistency.

In BALCO, the Supreme Court rendered a landmark judgment that firmly established the territoriality principle underpinning Part I of the Act. While primarily concerned with the jurisdiction of Indian courts over arbitrations seated outside India, the judgment laid the groundwork for a seat-centric approach. The Court clarified that the “place” of arbitration is the juridical seat, and it is this designation that determines the applicable procedural law and the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts.

The subsequent decision in Hardy Exploration introduced a measure of uncertainty. In that case, the arbitration agreement provided that the arbitration proceedings would be held in Kuala Lumpur, but the governing law of the contract was Indian law. The Court held that merely designating a location for hearings did not, in itself, make that location the juridical seat; something more was required to infer that the parties had chosen Kuala Lumpur as the seat.

The Brahmani River case, by contrast, adopted a more robust approach. There, the agreement provided that arbitration proceedings would be held at Bhubaneswar. The Court held that this designation of a specific location, coupled with the fact that the contract was governed by Indian law, was sufficient to conclude that Bhubaneswar was the juridical seat of the arbitration.

The tension between Hardy Exploration and Brahmani River has not been fully resolved. The former requires an additional inference to elevate a named location to a seat; the latter treats a named location, in the absence of contrary indicia, as the seat itself. This inconsistency continues to trouble practitioners and parties drafting arbitration agreements.

III. Conclusion: The Path to Clarity

The doctrinal uncertainty in this area carries real costs for parties to arbitration agreements. Uncertainty as to which court possesses supervisory jurisdiction can result in parallel proceedings before courts in different jurisdictions, with attendant delay, expense, and the risk of inconsistent decisions. It can undermine the efficacy of the arbitral process and deter parties from choosing India as a destination for international commercial arbitration.

The trajectory of the more recent decisions, particularly Brahmani River and the subsequent judgment in BGS SGS Soma JV v. NHPC Ltd. (2020), suggests a movement toward a seat-centric approach more closely aligned with international arbitral practice. In BGS SGS Soma, a Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court clarified that the principles in BALCO and Brahmani River represent the correct position in law and that Hardy Exploration must be read in light of these subsequent pronouncements.

The Court emphasized that the designation of a place in the arbitration agreement, combined with a governing law clause, would ordinarily be sufficient to establish that place as the juridical seat.

This clarification is welcome, but it does not eliminate the underlying ambiguity in Section 20 of the Act. The ideal solution lies in legislative reform: an express statutory distinction between the “seat” and the “venue,” with clear rules for determining each. Pending such reform, the courts must continue to develop a consistent and predictable jurisprudence, one that respects party autonomy, gives effect to the parties' intentions as expressed in their agreement, and aligns Indian arbitration law with the best international practice.

Only then can India fully realize its ambition of becoming a leading hub for international commercial arbitration.
