The Enforceability of Digital Contracts under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam

April 2025

I. Introduction

The growth of digital commerce has significantly altered the way contracts are formed. Physical signatures and paper documents are no longer the primary mechanisms through which agreements are concluded. Consumers and businesses now routinely enter into contracts through website clicks, browsewrap notices, and electronic signatures. A user purchasing software, subscribing to a streaming service, or accepting terms of use often manifests assent through a mouse click or by continuing to browse a website.

This transformation has progressed more rapidly than the statutory framework governing contract formation and evidence. The Indian Contract Act, 1872 predates digital communication by more than a century. Although the Information Technology Act, 2000 introduced legal recognition of electronic transactions, the recently enacted Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 replaces the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 and introduces significant changes regarding the proof of electronic records.

Section 63 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam corresponds broadly to the earlier Section 65B of the Evidence Act but includes clarifications concerning what constitutes primary evidence of electronic records. These changes have important implications for the enforceability of digital contracts.

This article examines the treatment of clickwrap, browsewrap, and signwrap agreements under Indian law. It analyses the consent requirement under the Contract Act, the authentication framework under the Information Technology Act, and the evidentiary requirements introduced by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam.

II. Typologies of Digital Contracts

Digital contracts appear in several forms, each raising distinct questions about formation and enforceability.

Clickwrap agreements require users to manifest affirmative assent by clicking an "I accept" or "I agree" button. The terms are usually displayed before the click and the transaction cannot proceed without acceptance. This format closely resembles traditional contract formation and courts have generally enforced such agreements.

Browsewrap agreements attempt to bind users merely through their use of a website. The terms are typically accessible through hyperlinks and the user is not required to take affirmative action indicating acceptance. This structure raises serious questions about notice and consent because the user may not even be aware of the existence of contractual terms.

Signwrap agreements combine elements of both approaches. The user signs or clicks to accept a document that incorporates external terms by reference, often available through a website link.

Other forms include shrinkwrap agreements, commonly used in software packaging, where the contract is accepted upon opening the package, and webwrap agreements, where submission of data through a website indicates acceptance of terms accessible through hyperlinks.

III. The Contract Act Framework

Section 2 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 defines a contract as an agreement enforceable by law made with the free consent of parties competent to contract. Sections 4, 7, and 14 elaborate the requirements for communication of offer, acceptance, and free consent.

Section 4 provides that communication of an offer is complete when it comes to the knowledge of the person to whom it is made. In the digital environment this raises questions about whether terms accessible only through hyperlinks can be said to come to the knowledge of the user.

Section 7 requires acceptance to be absolute and unqualified and communicated to the proposer. Section 14 defines free consent as consent not caused by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake.

The Bombay High Court in Trimex International FZE Ltd. v. Vedanta Aluminium Ltd. emphasised that offer and acceptance must be unequivocal and unconditional. Similarly, the Supreme Court in LIC of India v. Consumer Education and Research Centre observed that acceptance must be voluntary and intentional.

These principles apply equally to digital contracts. Clickwrap arrangements generally satisfy the requirement of intentional acceptance. Browsewrap agreements, where acceptance is inferred from continued browsing, present more serious difficulties.

IV. The Information Technology Act Framework

The Information Technology Act, 2000 provides the statutory foundation for electronic transactions in India.

Section 4 provides that where any law requires information to be in writing, that requirement is satisfied if the information is available in an electronic record. Section 5 recognises the validity of electronic signatures.

Section 10A is particularly significant. It provides that contracts formed through electronic means shall not be deemed unenforceable solely because they were concluded electronically.

Section 3 of the Act recognises electronic signatures authenticated through specified technological methods. These include digital signature certificates and other prescribed authentication mechanisms. In practice, where a user creates an account, enters credentials, and clicks "I agree," the combined actions may constitute adequate authentication.

While the IT Act validates electronic transactions, it does not prescribe how such transactions must be proved in court. That question is governed by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam.

V. The BSA Framework: Sections 62 and 63

The principal challenge in enforcing digital contracts lies in proof rather than formation. A party seeking to enforce a clickwrap agreement must demonstrate that the contract was formed, that the defendant was the person who accepted the terms, and that the terms presented at the time of acceptance are the terms now relied upon.

Section 63 of the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam corresponds to Section 65B of the earlier Evidence Act. It provides that electronic records produced by a computer are admissible as documents provided specified conditions are satisfied.

Sub-section (4) requires a certificate identifying the electronic record, describing the manner of its production, and certifying its accuracy. The certificate must be signed by a person occupying a responsible official position in relation to the device or system that produced the record.

In Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer, the Supreme Court held that the certification requirement under Section 65B constitutes a complete code for the admissibility of electronic evidence. Oral evidence cannot substitute for the required certificate.

The Court reaffirmed this position in Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal. It clarified that the certificate requirement is mandatory but may be satisfied later with the permission of the court, provided the hearing has not concluded.

VI. Clarifications on Primary Evidence

The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam introduces clarifications concerning what constitutes primary evidence of electronic records.

Explanation 4 to Section 57 provides that where electronic records are stored simultaneously or sequentially in multiple files, each such file constitutes primary evidence. Explanation 6 provides that video recordings simultaneously stored and transmitted electronically are primary evidence. Explanation 7 provides that electronic records stored in multiple storage locations within a computer system may each be treated as primary evidence.

These provisions recognise that electronic records do not have a single identifiable original in the way physical documents do. Multiple copies may exist simultaneously as part of normal computer operation.

Section 63 also requires the certificate to include the hash value of the electronic record. A hash value changes if even a minor alteration is made to the document, thereby assisting in detecting tampering.

VII. Practical Implications for Digital Contracting

The evidentiary framework established by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam creates several practical implications for businesses.

Businesses should design their digital contracting systems with evidentiary requirements in mind. Records must be maintained showing what terms were presented, when they were presented, and what action the user took in response.

Clickwrap agreements should be preferred over browsewrap arrangements. Clear affirmative acceptance provides stronger evidence of contract formation.

Version control of contractual terms is also essential. Businesses must be able to demonstrate which version of the terms was presented at the time of acceptance.

Where electronic signatures are used, compliance with Section 3 of the Information Technology Act is necessary to ensure proper authentication.

VIII. Conclusion

Digital contracts have become an essential component of modern commerce. Indian law addresses them through the combined operation of the Indian Contract Act, the Information Technology Act, and the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam.

The Contract Act supplies the principles of formation. The Information Technology Act validates electronic transactions. The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam governs how such transactions must be proved in court.

The most significant challenge lies in the evidentiary requirements of Section 63 of the BSA. Businesses must maintain reliable electronic records, preserve system logs, and ensure that certification requirements can be satisfied if disputes arise.

The clarifications introduced by the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam regarding primary evidence represent an attempt to adapt evidentiary law to the realities of digital information systems. Further judicial interpretation will likely refine these principles as digital contracting continues to evolve.
