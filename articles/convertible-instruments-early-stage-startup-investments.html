Convertible Instruments in Early-Stage Startup Investments: The Indian Statutory Framework

2026

I. Introduction

The earliest stage of a startup's commercial life presents a structural difficulty for investment law: the company has no reliable valuation, no revenue history, and often no tangible assets, yet capital must flow. Traditional equity financing requires agreement on a share price, while conventional debt financing imposes interest obligations and repayment deadlines that pre-revenue ventures can rarely sustain. To resolve this tension, early-stage investors and founders have adopted instruments that defer valuation to a future financing round while allowing capital to be received and deployed in the present.

In India, this problem has been addressed through a combination of statutory innovation and regulatory accommodation. The Companies Act, 2013, the Companies (Acceptance of Deposits) Rules, 2014, and the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-Debt Instruments) Rules, 2019 together constitute the framework within which convertible instruments in early-stage investments must be structured. This article examines each of the principal instruments available under Indian law: the convertible note recognised under the Deposits Rules, the compulsorily convertible preference share (CCPS), and the compulsorily convertible debenture (CCD). It then considers the cross-border compliance architecture and the doctrinal tensions that the current framework leaves unresolved.

II. The Startup as a Legal Category: DPIIT Recognition

The availability of the convertible note as a defined and regulated instrument turns, in the first instance, on whether a company qualifies as a "startup" within the meaning assigned by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT). Recognition under the DPIIT framework is not merely administrative; it is the gateway to statutory reliefs that would otherwise be unavailable to the issuing company.

A private limited company incorporated under the Companies Act, 2013 may seek DPIIT recognition if it was incorporated not more than ten years before the date of application, its annual turnover has not exceeded one hundred crore rupees in any preceding financial year, and it is working towards innovation, development, or improvement of products, processes, or services, or has a scalable business model with high potential for employment generation or wealth creation. Recognition is granted by certificate, and only recognised entities may issue convertible notes as defined instruments under the Deposits Rules. A company that fails to obtain or maintain DPIIT recognition cannot avail itself of the deposit exemption under Rule 2(1)(c)(xvii) of the Companies (Acceptance of Deposits) Rules, 2014. Founders who accept investment through convertible note instruments without first securing DPIIT recognition may inadvertently create a deposit-acceptance default under Sections 73 to 76A of the Companies Act, 2013, which impose stringent disclosure, repayment, and reserve requirements. The eligibility condition is accordingly foundational; the entire architecture of the convertible note as a permissible instrument rests upon it.

III. The Convertible Note under the Deposits Rules

Rule 2(1)(c)(xvii) of the Companies (Acceptance of Deposits) Rules, 2014 defines a convertible note as an instrument evidencing receipt of money initially as debt, which is either repayable at the option of the holder or convertible into equity shares within a period not exceeding ten years from the date of issue. The central value of this definition lies in the exemption it creates: amounts received by a DPIIT-recognised startup by way of a convertible note, in a minimum single tranche of twenty-five lakh rupees, are not treated as "deposits" for the purposes of the Companies Act. Without this exemption, the receipt of such funds would attract the deposit regime, which is structurally unsuited to pre-revenue ventures.

The issuance of convertible notes engages Section 62(3) of the Companies Act, 2013, which requires a special resolution of shareholders before debt instruments convertible into equity may be issued. The company must hold an extraordinary general meeting and file Form MGT-14 with the Registrar of Companies within thirty days of passing the special resolution. The convertible note agreement must set out the terms of conversion with sufficient specificity, including the trigger events, the conversion mechanics, and the applicable pricing formula.

The convertible note agreement typically specifies three commercial parameters that define the economics of conversion: a) the valuation cap, which limits the conversion price by setting a ceiling on the startup's pre-money valuation for purposes of conversion, regardless of the actual valuation determined at the trigger round; b) the discount rate, which entitles the noteholder to convert at a price below the price paid by incoming investors in the qualifying financing round, typically ranging between fifteen and twenty-five per cent; and c) the maturity date, upon which the outstanding note balance, if not converted, must be repaid with applicable interest. These commercial terms are creatures of contract rather than statute, but their interaction with the FEMA pricing requirements at the point of conversion introduces a compliance dimension that purely contractual drafting cannot fully address, and which is examined further below.

IV. Compulsorily Convertible Instruments: CCPS and CCD

Beyond the convertible note, early-stage investment in India is frequently structured through two instruments that are classified as equity from the moment of issuance: the compulsorily convertible preference share (CCPS) and the compulsorily convertible debenture (CCD). Both instruments are recognised as equity instruments under the Foreign Exchange Management (Non-Debt Instruments) Rules, 2019, making them the instruments of choice in rounds involving foreign investment, where the characterisation of an instrument as debt or equity has direct and material regulatory consequences.

The CCPS is issued as a preference share carrying a defined, typically nominal, preferential dividend, and must convert into equity shares within a period specified at the time of issuance. Section 55 of the Companies Act, 2013 governs the issuance and conversion of preference shares. It permits a company to issue preference shares convertible into equity shares, subject to a maximum period of twenty years for infrastructure projects and ten years for all other companies. For early-stage startups, conversion periods are usually tied to a qualifying financing round rather than a fixed date. The terms of a CCPS instrument typically provide for a liquidation preference, which entitles the holder to recover its investment before ordinary shareholders in a liquidation, dissolution, or deemed liquidation event, and for anti-dilution adjustments, which protect the holder against the issuance of equity at a price lower than the price at which the CCPS was subscribed. These economic protections are negotiated at the term sheet stage and embedded in the articles of association of the company, as modified by a shareholders' agreement.

The procedural requirements for issuing CCPS at the company law level include: a) a special resolution under Section 55 read with Section 62 of the Companies Act authorising the issuance and specifying the terms of conversion; b) the filing of Form MGT-14 with the Registrar of Companies within thirty days; c) the modification of the company's articles of association to reflect the special rights attached to the CCPS, if the existing articles do not accommodate them; and d) the filing of Form SH-7 with the Registrar upon increase of authorised share capital, where necessary to accommodate the new class of shares. Each of these steps must be completed before the CCPS is allotted, and any allotment made without the necessary shareholder authorisation is void ab initio.

The CCD takes the form of a debenture that mandatorily converts into equity upon the occurrence of a specified trigger or at the end of a defined term. Section 71 of the Companies Act, 2013 governs the issuance of debentures, and Rule 18 of the Companies (Share Capital and Debentures) Rules, 2014 sets out the procedural requirements. For early-stage investments where the number of debenture holders is small, the debenture trustee requirement ordinarily does not apply, as it is triggered only where debentures are issued to more than five hundred persons. The CCD finds application in structured early-stage investments where investors seek the legal character of a debt instrument with mandatory conversion into equity, and it is occasionally used in bridge financing rounds between two priced equity rounds.

The compulsory nature of conversion is the defining legal characteristic of both the CCPS and the CCD. A preference share or debenture that is merely optionally convertible does not qualify as an equity instrument under the NDI Rules; only instruments that must convert satisfy the statutory definition. This distinction is of first importance in transactions involving foreign investors. An optionally convertible instrument received by a non-resident is treated as external commercial borrowing under the FEMA framework, attracting the end-use restrictions, all-in cost ceilings, and reporting obligations applicable to ECBs rather than to FDI. Any optionality inadvertently introduced into what is intended to be a compulsorily convertible instrument risks reclassifying the investment entirely, with the attendant consequences of a FEMA contravention.

V. Debt, Equity, and the Classification Problem in Distress and Taxation

The convertible note is unique among the instruments discussed here in its explicit dual character: it is received as debt and may, but need not, convert to equity. This hybrid nature generates doctrinal consequences that are not always appreciated when the instrument is negotiated.

As debt, the convertible note accrues interest, which creates a tax withholding obligation where the noteholder is a non-resident. Under Section 195 of the Income Tax Act, 1961, payments of interest to persons resident outside India attract withholding at applicable treaty or statutory rates. Founders who treat convertible notes as equity-deferred arrangements without accounting for the interest accrual and its withholding tax consequences may inadvertently create tax defaults, particularly in rounds where the note carries a nominal rate that nonetheless constitutes taxable interest income for the holder.

A more consequential problem arises in insolvency. If a DPIIT-recognised startup were to enter corporate insolvency resolution proceedings under the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 before a convertible note has converted, the noteholder's status as a creditor rather than a shareholder gives it the right to file an application under Section 7 of the Code as a financial creditor. The convertibility feature does not alter the creditor status of the holder prior to conversion. This consequence is rarely considered at the time convertible notes are negotiated, but it is a doctrinally significant implication of treating the instrument as debt in its pre-conversion phase. It places the noteholder in a materially superior position to ordinary equity shareholders in a distressed scenario, a result that may not reflect the economic bargain the parties intended when the note was structured as a deferred equity investment.

The CCPS and CCD, as equity instruments from inception, do not carry the same pre-conversion creditor risk. However, the liquidation preference typically attached to CCPS creates its own priority question in a winding-up or deemed liquidation: whether the contractual liquidation preference in a shareholders' agreement constitutes an enforceable priority right against the company in insolvency, or whether it is merely a right inter se among shareholders, remains a question on which Indian courts have not definitively pronounced. The legal position implies that founders and investors should exercise care in drafting the liquidation preference clause to ensure its intended enforceability is not undermined by the insolvency framework.

VI. Cross-Border Investment and FEMA Compliance

The most substantial compliance burden in early-stage convertible instrument transactions arises when foreign investment is involved. The Foreign Exchange Management (Non-Debt Instruments) Rules, 2019 permit a person resident outside India to subscribe to convertible notes issued by an Indian startup company for an amount of twenty-five lakh rupees or more in a single tranche. Investment must be received through inward remittance via banking channels or by debit to NRE, FCNR(B), or escrow accounts maintained by the investor. Startups in sectors where foreign direct investment requires government approval cannot issue convertible notes to non-residents without first obtaining such approval.

Issuance of a convertible note to a foreign investor must be reported on Form CN on the FIRMS portal of the Reserve Bank of India within thirty days of the date of issuance. For CCPS and CCD issuances to foreign investors, the allotment must be reported on Form FC-GPR within thirty days of allotment. Upon conversion of any of these instruments into equity shares, a further Form FC-GPR filing is required within thirty days of the consequent allotment. Failure to file within prescribed periods constitutes a contravention under Section 13 of the Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999, and is subject to compounding proceedings before the Reserve Bank.

The pricing framework applicable to conversion is among the most practically consequential aspects of the FEMA regime. Under the NDI Rules, the conversion price of a convertible note must not be less than the fair value of the equity shares as determined at the time of issuance of the note, certified by a SEBI-registered merchant banker or a chartered accountant. This pricing floor interacts with the commercially negotiated valuation cap and discount rate in ways that can produce a compliance problem at conversion. If the application of a discount rate or valuation cap would produce a conversion price below the FEMA-mandated fair value floor, the parties cannot simply apply the contractual formula without a regulatory contravention. The legal position requires that conversion be effected at no less than the fair value floor, and the difference between the contractually agreed economics and the regulatory requirement must be reconciled before the allotment proceeds.

For CCPS and CCD, the position is somewhat clearer. The Reserve Bank of India has clarified that the conversion of CCPS or CCD into equity shares does not require a fresh valuation at the time of conversion if the conversion formula was determined and disclosed at the time of issuance. This provides greater regulatory predictability for CCPS and CCD investors than for convertible noteholders, where the conversion price is often linked to a future priced round and cannot always be fully pre-determined. The relative clarity available for CCPS and CCD conversion pricing is one reason why sophisticated foreign investors frequently prefer these instruments over the convertible note in Indian early-stage transactions, notwithstanding the greater procedural complexity involved in their issuance.

VII. Conclusion

The statutory framework governing convertible instruments in early-stage Indian startup investment reflects a considered attempt to accommodate the commercial needs of early-stage financing within the constraints of a regulatory architecture that was not originally designed with pre-revenue ventures in mind. The convertible note, as exempted under the Deposits Rules, provides a genuinely useful instrument for domestic seed investment in DPIIT-recognised entities. The CCPS and CCD, as recognised equity instruments under the NDI Rules, provide the preferred structure for foreign investment, with greater regulatory predictability at the conversion stage.

Notwithstanding these accommodations, significant doctrinal tensions remain unresolved. The pre-conversion creditor status of a convertible noteholder in insolvency is an under-examined risk that sits in tension with the economic character of the instrument as deferred equity. The interaction between commercially negotiated discount rates and the FEMA pricing floor at conversion creates a structural gap between contractual freedom and regulatory compliance that the current framework addresses inadequately. The enforceability of liquidation preferences against the company in insolvency proceedings, as distinct from their enforceability inter se among shareholders, remains judicially untested in the Indian context.

Each of these tensions bears on the rights and obligations of investors and founders at moments when those rights are most likely to be contested. The legal position implies that parties to convertible instrument transactions should address these questions with deliberate attention at the drafting stage rather than treat them as peripheral concerns. The growth of the Indian startup ecosystem is likely to press these questions upon the regulators and courts with increasing force, and a more definitive framework would serve the interests of all participants in the early-stage investment market.
