DPDP Act: Contractual Liability between Fiduciaries and Processors

March 2026

I. Introduction

The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, establishes a comprehensive framework for data protection in India. Central to this framework is the distinction between Data Fiduciaries and Data Processors. A Data Fiduciary determines the purpose and means of processing personal data. A Data Processor processes personal data on behalf of a Data Fiduciary. This distinction, while apparently straightforward, has significant implications for contractual drafting and risk allocation.

Unlike the European Union's General Data Protection Regulation, which imposes direct statutory obligations on both controllers and processors, the DPDP Act adopts a fiduciary-centric model. The Data Fiduciary bears primary responsibility for compliance with the law's requirements and remains liable for the acts and omissions of the Data Processors it appoints. This legislative choice places considerable weight on the contractual arrangements between fiduciaries and processors. The Data Processing Agreement becomes not merely a commercial document but a critical instrument for risk management and regulatory compliance.

The DPDP Rules, 2025, notified on November 13, 2025, provide detailed specifications for these contractual arrangements. With a staggered implementation period of up to eighteen months, organisations have until May 2027 to achieve full compliance. This article examines the mandatory contractual clauses required under the DPDP Rules, 2025, focusing particularly on breach notification timelines and liability allocation for third-party vendors. It analyses the statutory framework governing fiduciary-processor relationships, considers the practical challenges of implementing effective DPAs, and evaluates the extent to which contractual provisions can shield fiduciaries from liability arising from processor negligence.

II. The Statutory Framework

The DPDP Act, 2023, establishes the basic architecture of fiduciary-processor relationships through several key provisions. Section 8(2) provides that a Data Fiduciary may engage a Data Processor to process personal data on its behalf under a valid contract. Section 8(5) requires both fiduciaries and processors to implement reasonable security safeguards to prevent personal data breaches. Section 8(6) mandates that in the event of a personal data breach, the Data Fiduciary must notify the Data Protection Board of India and each affected Data Principal.

The Act does not impose direct statutory obligations on Data Processors. Instead, processors are bound primarily through their contracts with fiduciaries. This approach reflects a policy choice to centralise accountability with the entity that determines the purposes and means of processing. However, the practical effect is that fiduciaries must exercise careful oversight over their processors, as regulatory penalties for non-compliance may be imposed on the fiduciary even where the breach originates with the processor.

The DPDP Rules, 2025, provide further specificity. Rule 6 defines "reasonable security safeguards" and details mandatory measures that must be implemented. These include technical measures such as encryption at rest and in transit, access controls, network segmentation, and logging of data access and processing activities. Organisational measures include staff training, background checks, and incident response protocols. Rule 8 imposes additional data retention obligations, requiring fiduciaries to retain logs of data processing activities for at least one year and to provide forty-eight hours notice before deletion of personal data.

The Rules were notified on November 13, 2025, with phased implementation over eighteen months. Organisations are required to achieve full compliance by May 2027. The Data Protection Board of India has been established immediately with four members to oversee implementation and enforcement.

III. Essential Elements of a Data Processing Agreement

A Data Processing Agreement under the DPDP Act serves multiple functions. It documents the fiduciary's instructions to the processor, defines the scope and limitations of processing, establishes security and sub-processing controls, and creates audit, cooperation, and deletion obligations. The DPA is the primary mechanism through which the fiduciary's statutory obligations are flowed down to the processor.

Several elements are essential to an effective DPA. First, the agreement must clearly define the scope of processing, including the purposes for which data may be processed, the categories of personal data involved, the categories of data principals, and the duration of processing. This purpose limitation is critical because processing beyond the scope of the fiduciary's instructions may render the processor's actions unlawful and expose both parties to liability.

Second, the DPA must specify security obligations in accordance with Rule 6 of the DPDP Rules. Processors must implement technical and organisational measures commensurate with the risks associated with processing. These measures must include encryption, access controls, logging, and regular testing of security systems. The DPA should require the processor to maintain confidentiality obligations for its personnel and to ensure that any person authorised to process personal data has committed themselves to confidentiality.

Third, the DPA must address sub-processing. Processors frequently engage third-party sub-processors for activities such as cloud hosting, analytics, or customer support. The fiduciary remains responsible for all processing carried out on its behalf, including by sub-processors. Accordingly, the DPA should require the processor to obtain the fiduciary's prior written consent before engaging any sub-processor, and to impose equivalent obligations on such sub-processors by way of a further contract. The processor should remain fully liable to the fiduciary for the acts and omissions of its sub-processors.

Fourth, the DPA must include audit rights. The fiduciary must have the ability to verify the processor's compliance with its obligations. This may include rights to conduct on-site inspections, to review audit reports prepared by independent third parties, and to obtain all information necessary to demonstrate compliance. The frequency and scope of audits should be proportionate to the risks associated with the processing.

Fifth, the DPA must address data retention and deletion in accordance with Rule 8. Upon termination of the processing relationship, the processor must, at the fiduciary's choice, return or delete all personal data, unless retention is required by law. The processor must retain logs of processing activities for at least one year and must provide forty-eight hours notice before deletion of any personal data. A certification of deletion should be provided to the fiduciary.

IV. Breach Notification

One of the most operationally significant provisions of the DPDP Rules is the requirement for prompt breach notification. Section 8(6) of the Act requires fiduciaries to notify the Data Protection Board and affected data principals of any personal data breach. Rule 7 specifies that such notification must be made within seventy-two hours of the fiduciary becoming aware of the breach.

This timeline creates particular challenges where processing is carried out by a third-party processor. The processor may be the first to detect a breach, and the fiduciary's ability to comply with the seventy-two hour notification requirement depends entirely on the processor's prompt reporting. Accordingly, the DPA must impose clear and enforceable obligations on the processor to notify the fiduciary immediately upon becoming aware of any breach.

The notification obligation should not be limited to confirmed breaches. Many incidents require investigation to determine whether a breach has in fact occurred. The DPA should require the processor to notify the fiduciary of any security incident that could reasonably result in a personal data breach, even where confirmation is pending. This allows the fiduciary to assess the incident and prepare any required notifications while the processor conducts its investigation.

The DPA should also specify the information that the processor must provide in its notification. This should include, at a minimum, a description of the nature of the breach, the categories and approximate number of data principals affected, the likely consequences of the breach, and the measures taken or proposed to be taken to address the breach. The processor should be required to cooperate fully with the fiduciary's investigation and remediation efforts.

Failure to include adequate breach notification provisions in the DPA exposes the fiduciary to significant risk. If the processor delays reporting a breach, the fiduciary may miss the seventy-two hour notification deadline and become subject to penalty. The regulator is unlikely to accept the processor's delay as an excuse, as the fiduciary retains primary responsibility for compliance.

V. Grievance Redressal

Rule 14 of the DPDP Rules imposes significant obligations regarding grievance redressal. The Data Fiduciary must establish a mechanism for receiving and addressing complaints from data principals and must respond to any grievance within ninety days of its receipt. Contact details of the Data Protection Officer or other designated officer must be published.

Where processing is carried out by a third-party processor, the fiduciary must ensure that the processor has adequate mechanisms to handle grievances that arise from its processing activities. The DPA should require the processor to forward any grievance received directly to the fiduciary promptly and to cooperate in the investigation and resolution of such grievances.

The ninety-day timeline runs from the date the grievance is received by the fiduciary. If a grievance is first received by the processor, the fiduciary's timeline may be affected by delays in forwarding. The DPA should therefore require immediate forwarding of any grievance and should specify that the processor remains responsible for any failure to meet the timeline caused by its delay.

VI. Liability Allocation

The question of liability allocation between fiduciaries and processors is among the most contested in DPA negotiations. Processors seek to limit their exposure, while fiduciaries seek to ensure that they can recover losses arising from processor negligence.

The DPDP Act does not address this question directly. As noted, the Act adopts a fiduciary-centric model, meaning that the regulator will look first to the fiduciary in the event of non-compliance. If a processor's act or omission causes a breach, the fiduciary may be required to pay a penalty, but the Act does not create a statutory right of contribution or indemnity against the processor. Such rights must be created by contract.

Indemnity clauses serve this function. A well-drafted indemnity clause requires the processor to compensate the fiduciary for losses arising from the processor's breach of the DPA or from its negligent acts or omissions. This may include not only direct losses but also any penalties imposed on the fiduciary by the Data Protection Board. Indemnity clauses should be carefully negotiated to ensure that they provide adequate protection without being so one-sided as to be commercially unacceptable.

Processors typically seek to include limitation of liability clauses. These clauses cap the processor's liability at a specified amount, often linked to the fees paid under the DPA. From the fiduciary's perspective, such caps may be inadequate where a breach exposes the fiduciary to penalties of up to two hundred fifty crore rupees. The fiduciary must assess whether the processor's proposed cap is commensurate with the risks associated with the processing.

Where the processor's standard liability cap is unacceptable, the fiduciary may propose alternative structures. These may include higher caps for breaches caused by the processor's gross negligence or wilful misconduct, insurance requirements that provide additional coverage, or tiered caps based on the sensitivity of the data being processed.

VIII. Conclusion

The DPDP Act's fiduciary-centric model places primary responsibility for data protection compliance on Data Fiduciaries, while Data Processors are bound primarily through contract. This legislative choice makes the Data Processing Agreement a critical instrument for risk management and regulatory compliance.

The DPDP Rules, 2025, notified in November 2025 with phased implementation until May 2027, provide detailed requirements for these agreements. Effective DPAs must address several essential elements including scope limitation, security obligations, controls on sub-processing, audit rights, retention obligations, breach notification provisions, and grievance mechanisms. Liability allocation through indemnity and limitation clauses must be carefully negotiated to balance commercial realities with regulatory risk.
