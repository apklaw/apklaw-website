Arbitral Seat and Venue: The Distinction and Its Doctrinal Significance in Indian Arbitration Law

November 2025

I. Introduction

The distinction between the juridical seat of an arbitration and the physical venue at which hearings are conducted has been a source of considerable doctrinal difficulty in Indian arbitration law. The significance of the distinction lies in its profound consequences for the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts: the courts of the seat possess exclusive curial jurisdiction over the arbitral proceedings, while the courts at the venue, being merely a convenient location for hearings, do not. Despite decades of arbitration jurisprudence, this seemingly elementary distinction continues to generate litigation, reflecting persistent ambiguities in the interpretation of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996.

This article examines the distinction between seat and venue in Indian arbitration law. It analyses the statutory framework under Section 20 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, traces the judicial treatment of the distinction in leading Supreme Court decisions, and considers whether recent clarifications have resolved the underlying uncertainties.

II. The Seat and Venue Distinction in Principle

The concept of the juridical seat is a creature of private international law and not merely a description of geography. It is the legal home of the arbitration, a construct that may bear no relation to where the arbitrators actually sit. The seat determines the lex arbitri, the law governing the arbitral procedure, and confers jurisdiction on the courts of that jurisdiction to supervise the arbitration.

This distinction is well established in English arbitration law. In Naviera Amazonica Peruana SA v. Compania Internacional de Seguros del Peru, the English Court of Appeal held that the selection of a location for hearings does not necessarily determine the juridical seat. Similarly, in Channel Tunnel Group Ltd. v. Balfour Beatty Ltd., the House of Lords reaffirmed that the seat is a legal concept distinct from the physical location of proceedings.

III. The Statutory Framework: Section 20 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996

The Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 employs the phrase “place of arbitration” without clearly distinguishing between the juridical seat and the venue of hearings. Section 20(1) provides that the parties are free to agree on the place of arbitration, while Section 20(2) permits the arbitral tribunal to determine the place failing such agreement. Section 20(3) allows the tribunal to meet at any place it considers appropriate for hearings or consultation.

This language creates ambiguity. The statute does not explicitly distinguish between the juridical seat and locations used merely for convenience. Consequently, disputes have arisen regarding whether a designated location constitutes the seat of arbitration or merely the venue.

IV. The Supreme Court’s Treatment: BALCO and the Seat-Centric Approach

The modern doctrinal foundation was established in Bharat Aluminium Co. v. Kaiser Aluminium Technical Services Inc. (2012), commonly referred to as BALCO. The Supreme Court affirmed the territoriality principle and held that Part I of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act applies only to arbitrations seated in India.

The Court clarified that the “place” of arbitration under Section 20(1) refers to the juridical seat. The designation of the seat determines the procedural law governing the arbitration and the supervisory jurisdiction of the courts.

V. The Hardy Exploration and Brahmani River Divergence

Subsequent decisions revealed inconsistencies in applying the seat-venue distinction. In Union of India v. Hardy Exploration and Production (India) Inc. (2018), the Supreme Court held that merely designating Kuala Lumpur as the location for arbitration hearings did not automatically make it the juridical seat.

In contrast, the Court in Brahmani River Pellets Ltd. v. Kamachi Industries Ltd. (2019) treated the designation of Bhubaneswar as the place of arbitration as sufficient to establish it as the juridical seat.

VI. The BGS SGS Soma Clarification

The Supreme Court sought to reconcile these decisions in BGS SGS Soma JV v. NHPC Ltd. (2020). The Court reaffirmed the seat-centric approach adopted in BALCO and clarified that where an arbitration agreement designates a place of arbitration, that place will ordinarily be regarded as the juridical seat unless there are clear indications to the contrary.

The decision strengthened doctrinal clarity by emphasising that party intention and the designation of a specific place in the arbitration agreement are decisive factors in determining the seat.

VII. Doctrinal Analysis and Unresolved Questions

The trajectory of Supreme Court jurisprudence reveals increasing clarity regarding the distinction between seat and venue. The courts now recognise the seat as the legal home of arbitration and the determinant of curial jurisdiction.

Nevertheless, the statutory language of Section 20 continues to employ the phrase “place of arbitration” without distinguishing between seat and venue. This linguistic ambiguity leaves room for continued litigation.

VIII. Conclusion

The distinction between the juridical seat and the physical venue of arbitration is fundamental to the arbitral process. It determines which courts exercise supervisory jurisdiction and which procedural law governs the arbitration.

Indian arbitration law has gradually developed a coherent seat-centric framework through judicial interpretation. Yet the statutory language remains ambiguous, and legislative clarification distinguishing the seat from the venue would further strengthen doctrinal certainty.
