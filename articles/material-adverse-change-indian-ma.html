Material Adverse Change Clauses in Indian M&A: The Impossibility Standard and Its Limits

March 2026

I. Introduction

Material Adverse Change clauses, commonly referred to as MAC clauses, are a standard feature of mergers and acquisition agreements. Their purpose is to allocate the risk of unforeseen events occurring between signing and closing. A well-drafted MAC clause permits the acquirer to withdraw from the transaction if certain adverse events materially affect the target company's business, financial condition, or prospects. In principle, these clauses serve as a critical risk allocation tool.

In practice, however, the enforceability of MAC clauses in India has been significantly constrained by judicial interpretation. Indian courts have consistently read MAC clauses through the lens of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, which governs the doctrine of frustration. This has resulted in an exceptionally high threshold for invocation. The question of whether financial distress, including insolvency proceedings against the target, qualifies as a MAC remains particularly unsettled. This article examines the interaction between MAC clauses and the moratorium provisions of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016, and considers whether financial distress can ever satisfy the stringent standards set by Indian courts.

II. The Nature and Function of MAC Clauses

MAC clauses operate in the interim period between the signing of an acquisition agreement and its closing. During this period, the acquirer assumes the risk that the target's business may deteriorate. A MAC clause provides a contractual mechanism to address such deterioration. If a material adverse change occurs, the acquirer may be entitled to terminate the agreement or refuse to close.

The drafting of MAC clauses varies considerably. Some clauses define materiality by reference to objective metrics such as earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortisation or profit after tax over a particular period. Others use broader language referring to any event that has a material adverse effect on the business, financial condition, or prospects of the target company. Sellers often resist broadly worded clauses and seek to limit MAC definitions to events that are both objectively verifiable and outside the ordinary course of business.

The essential difficulty with MAC clauses lies in the concept of materiality itself. What constitutes a material adverse change is inherently subjective and context-dependent. Courts across jurisdictions have struggled to develop consistent standards. In India, this difficulty is compounded by the judicial tendency to treat MAC clauses as subject to the doctrine of frustration under Section 56 of the Contract Act.

III. The Statutory Framework: Section 56 of the Contract Act

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, embodies the doctrine of frustration. It provides that a contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible or unlawful becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful. The section has been interpreted to cover not only physical impossibility but also situations where the fundamental basis of the contract is destroyed.

In Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954), the Supreme Court observed that the word "impossible" in Section 56 must not be interpreted in its literal sense. Rather, it refers to situations where the contract's performance becomes impractical as regards the object and purpose of what the parties had intended.

However, subsequent decisions have significantly narrowed the scope of Section 56. In Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (2017), the Supreme Court held that the performance of a contract is not discharged merely because it has become onerous for a party due to unforeseen events. The Court emphasised that it is imperative to prove that performance has become objectively impossible, not merely financially detrimental.

IV. The Leading Authority: Nirma Industries and the Impossibility Standard

The most significant decision on MAC clauses in the Indian context is Nirma Industries Ltd. v. SEBI (2013). Although this case arose in the context of public takeover regulations, its reasoning has shaped the judicial approach to MAC clauses more broadly.

Nirma Industries had entered into an agreement that triggered an obligation to make an open offer under the SEBI Takeover Regulations. After making the public announcement, Nirma discovered that the promoters of the target company had engaged in financial misrepresentation and fraud involving embezzlement of funds exceeding 350 crore rupees. Nirma applied to SEBI for withdrawal of the open offer, arguing that these circumstances constituted a material adverse change.

The Supreme Court rejected Nirma's application. Interpreting Regulation 27 of the 1997 Takeover Regulations, the Court held that withdrawal could be permitted only under conditions of impossibility of performance. The two expressly specified grounds in the regulation related to legal impossibility and natural impossibility. Applying the canon of ejusdem generis, the Court concluded that any other ground for withdrawal must also fall within the same genus of impossibility.

Despite Nirma's arguments that financial misrepresentation and fraud materially affected the transaction, the Court held that withdrawal was justified only in cases of impossibility. The fact that the target company's financial health had deteriorated, even due to fraud, did not meet this standard.

V. The Question of Financial Distress

The reasoning in Nirma Industries has been extended to cases involving financial distress more generally. In SEBI v. Akshya Infrastructure Pvt. Ltd. (2014), the Supreme Court held that an unjustifiable delay by SEBI in approving a takeover offer, which made the offer unfavourable for the acquirer, was insufficient to invoke frustration. The Court stated that economic difficulty does not come under the impossibility threshold because market fluctuations and financial strain have not been recognised as grounds for frustration of a contract.

Similarly, in Pramod Jain v. SEBI (2016), the Court held that a delay of two years by SEBI in approving an open offer did not justify withdrawal, even though the target company's financial health had deteriorated during those two years. The deterioration of financial condition, without more, did not render performance impossible.

VI. The Insolvency Dimension: IBC Moratorium

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016, introduces a new dimension to this analysis. Section 14 of the IBC imposes a moratorium on the institution or continuation of proceedings against a corporate debtor once insolvency proceedings are initiated. The moratorium prohibits the initiation of suits, execution proceedings, and other legal actions.

The question that arises is whether the initiation of insolvency proceedings against a target company, and the consequent moratorium, can constitute a MAC entitling the acquirer to withdraw from an acquisition agreement. The answer is not straightforward.

On one hand, the moratorium under Section 14 has significant practical consequences. The management of the target company is displaced and replaced by an insolvency professional. The target's assets are protected from creditor action. The resolution process may fundamentally alter the company's structure and ownership. These circumstances could plausibly be argued to constitute a material adverse change.

On the other hand, the judicial approach outlined above suggests that even these significant disruptions may not meet the impossibility standard. The moratorium does not render performance of the acquisition agreement impossible. The acquirer can still complete the purchase of shares, albeit subject to the constraints of the insolvency process. The transaction may become more difficult, more uncertain, or less attractive, but it does not become impossible.

The SEBI order in the matter of Jyoti Private Limited (2016) is instructive. In that case, the target company was registered as a sick industrial company with the Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction. The BIFR had ordered that status quo be maintained in terms of control of the company. The acquirers sought to withdraw their open offer, arguing that no change in control was feasible due to the pending BIFR proceedings.

SEBI rejected the request for withdrawal. Applying the reasoning in Nirma Industries, SEBI held that the completion of the open offer process was not impossible. The fact that change of control might require BIFR approval did not render the offer incapable of performance.

VII. Practical Implications

The judicial approach to MAC clauses carries significant implications for transaction structuring and documentation.

First, acquirers cannot rely on broadly worded MAC clauses to protect against financial deterioration of the target. The threshold for invocation is impossibility, not merely material adverse change.
