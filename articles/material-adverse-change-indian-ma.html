Material Adverse Change Clauses in Indian M&A: The Impossibility Standard and Its Limits

March 2026

I. Introduction

Material Adverse Change clauses, commonly referred to as MAC clauses, are a standard feature of merger and acquisition agreements. Their purpose is to allocate the risk of unforeseen events occurring between signing and closing. A properly drafted MAC clause permits the acquirer to withdraw from the transaction if certain adverse events materially affect the target company's business, financial condition, or prospects.

In principle, these clauses function as an important mechanism of risk allocation. In practice, however, their enforceability in India has been constrained by judicial interpretation. Indian courts have frequently interpreted MAC clauses through the lens of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, which embodies the doctrine of frustration. This has resulted in a very high threshold for invocation.

The question of whether financial distress, including insolvency proceedings against the target company, constitutes a material adverse change remains particularly unsettled. This article examines the interaction between MAC clauses and the moratorium provisions of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 and considers whether financial distress can ever satisfy the demanding standards articulated by Indian courts.

II. The Nature and Function of MAC Clauses

MAC clauses operate in the period between the signing of an acquisition agreement and its closing. During this interim period the acquirer assumes the risk that the target company's business condition may deteriorate. The clause provides a contractual mechanism allowing the acquirer to terminate the agreement or refuse to complete the transaction if a material adverse change occurs.

The drafting of MAC clauses varies widely. Some define materiality using objective financial metrics such as earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortisation or profit after tax over a defined period. Others employ broader formulations referring to any event that materially affects the business, financial condition, or prospects of the target company.

Sellers frequently resist broad formulations and attempt to limit MAC definitions to objectively verifiable events that fall outside the ordinary course of business. The underlying difficulty lies in the concept of materiality itself. Determining whether a change is material is inherently contextual and has produced inconsistent judicial approaches across jurisdictions.

In India this uncertainty is intensified by the judicial tendency to interpret MAC clauses through the doctrine of frustration under Section 56 of the Contract Act.

III. The Statutory Framework: Section 56 of the Contract Act

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 embodies the doctrine of frustration. It provides that a contract to perform an act which becomes impossible or unlawful after the contract is made becomes void when such impossibility arises.

The Supreme Court in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. held that the term "impossible" should not be confined to literal physical impossibility. The concept also includes situations where the fundamental basis of the contract has been destroyed and performance becomes impractical in relation to the purpose contemplated by the parties.

Subsequent decisions significantly narrowed this interpretation. In Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission, the Supreme Court emphasised that a contract is not discharged merely because performance has become economically burdensome. The party invoking frustration must demonstrate objective impossibility rather than financial disadvantage.

This distinction between impossibility and mere commercial difficulty has played a decisive role in the judicial interpretation of MAC clauses.

IV. The Leading Authority: Nirma Industries

The most significant decision concerning MAC clauses in the Indian context is Nirma Industries Ltd. v. SEBI. Although the dispute arose under the SEBI Takeover Regulations, its reasoning has influenced the broader judicial treatment of MAC provisions.

After announcing a public offer, Nirma discovered that the promoters of the target company had engaged in financial misrepresentation involving substantial embezzlement. Nirma sought permission to withdraw the open offer, arguing that these developments constituted a material adverse change.

The Supreme Court rejected the request. Interpreting Regulation 27 of the Takeover Regulations, the Court held that withdrawal could be permitted only where performance had become impossible. Applying the doctrine of ejusdem generis, the Court reasoned that the enumerated grounds for withdrawal related to legal or natural impossibility. Any additional ground must fall within the same category.

Even substantial financial misrepresentation by the promoters did not meet this standard. The Court concluded that deterioration of the target company's financial position did not render the acquisition impossible.

V. Financial Distress as a MAC

The reasoning in Nirma Industries has influenced later cases dealing with financial distress. In SEBI v. Akshya Infrastructure Pvt. Ltd., the Supreme Court held that delay by SEBI in approving an open offer did not justify withdrawal, even though the delay made the transaction economically unattractive for the acquirer.

Similarly, in Pramod Jain v. SEBI, the Court held that deterioration in the financial condition of the target company during regulatory delay did not amount to impossibility of performance.

These decisions illustrate the consistent judicial reluctance to treat financial hardship as a basis for avoiding contractual obligations. The courts have prioritised contractual certainty and have refused to allow parties to withdraw merely because the transaction has become economically unfavourable.

VI. The Insolvency Dimension

The Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016 introduces additional complexity. Section 14 of the Code imposes a moratorium once insolvency proceedings are initiated. This moratorium suspends suits, execution proceedings, and enforcement actions against the corporate debtor.

The initiation of insolvency proceedings may significantly affect the commercial attractiveness of an acquisition. Management control is transferred to an insolvency professional and the resolution process may alter the company's structure or ownership.

However, existing judicial reasoning suggests that these consequences may not satisfy the impossibility standard. The acquisition itself remains capable of performance. The acquirer may still complete the transaction subject to the insolvency process.

An instructive example appears in the SEBI order concerning Jyoti Private Limited. In that matter the target company had been declared sick under proceedings before the Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction. The acquirers sought to withdraw their open offer on the ground that change of control could not be implemented.

SEBI rejected the request, holding that the transaction was not impossible merely because regulatory approval was required. The reasoning suggests that the existence of insolvency proceedings alone would not necessarily justify invocation of a MAC clause.

VII. Practical Implications

The restrictive judicial approach to MAC clauses has important implications for transaction structuring.

First, broadly worded MAC clauses offer limited protection against deterioration of the target company's financial condition. The threshold for invocation approaches the standard of impossibility rather than mere material change.

Second, the withdrawal provisions under the Takeover Regulations have not been interpreted expansively. Regulatory authorities have generally applied the reasoning of Nirma Industries when considering requests for withdrawal.

Third, parties seeking meaningful protection should consider drafting clauses with objective financial triggers. For example, the agreement may permit termination if the target's net worth declines by a specified percentage before closing.

Fourth, insolvency risks should be addressed explicitly in transaction documentation. A clause dealing specifically with the initiation of insolvency proceedings against the target is more likely to be effective than a general MAC provision.

The Supreme Court decision in Gujarat Urja Vikas Nigam Ltd. v. Solar Semiconductor Power Company (India) Pvt. Ltd. reinforces the importance of precise drafting. The Court emphasised that parties must expressly allocate risks if they intend those risks to affect contractual obligations.

VIII. Conclusion

Indian courts have adopted a cautious approach to the interpretation of MAC clauses. By aligning these provisions with the doctrine of frustration under Section 56 of the Contract Act, courts have effectively imposed a demanding impossibility standard.

Financial deterioration, even when severe, rarely meets this threshold. The interaction between MAC clauses and insolvency proceedings under the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code remains relatively unexplored, but existing decisions suggest that insolvency alone may not render performance impossible.

For practitioners, the lesson is clear. General MAC clauses provide limited protection in the Indian legal environment. Parties must allocate risks through carefully drafted provisions that address specific contingencies rather than relying on broad formulations.
