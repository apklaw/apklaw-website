Material Adverse Change Clauses in Indian M&A: The Impossibility Standard and Its Limits

March 2026

I. Introduction

Material Adverse Change clauses, commonly referred to as MAC clauses, are a standard feature of mergers and acquisition agreements. Their purpose is to allocate the risk of unforeseen events occurring between signing and closing. A well-drafted MAC clause permits the acquirer to withdraw from the transaction if certain adverse events materially affect the target company's business, financial condition, or prospects. In principle, these clauses serve as a critical risk allocation tool in transaction documentation.

In practice, however, the enforceability of MAC clauses in India has been significantly constrained by judicial interpretation. Indian courts have consistently read MAC clauses through the lens of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, which governs the doctrine of frustration. This has resulted in an exceptionally high threshold for invocation. The question of whether financial distress, including insolvency proceedings against the target, qualifies as a MAC remains particularly unsettled. This article examines how Indian courts interpret MAC clauses in M&A contracts, analyses the leading authorities on withdrawal from transactions, and considers whether specific drafting can overcome the restrictive judicial approach.

II. MAC Clauses in M&A Transaction Documentation

MAC clauses operate in the interim period between the signing of an acquisition agreement and its closing. During this period, the acquirer assumes the risk that the target's business may deteriorate. A MAC clause provides a contractual mechanism to address such deterioration. If a material adverse change occurs, the acquirer may be entitled to terminate the agreement or refuse to close.

The drafting of MAC clauses in M&A contracts varies considerably. Some clauses define materiality by reference to objective metrics such as earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortisation or profit after tax over a particular period. Others use broader language referring to any event that has a material adverse effect on the business, financial condition, or prospects of the target company. Sellers often resist broadly worded clauses and seek to limit MAC definitions to events that are both objectively verifiable and outside the ordinary course of business.

MAC clauses typically include specific exclusions. Common carve-outs include changes in general economic or political conditions, changes affecting the industry generally, acts of war or terrorism, and compliance with laws. These exclusions are intended to prevent the acquirer from invoking the MAC clause based on events that affect all market participants rather than the target specifically. The burden of proving that a change falls within the MAC clause and outside any exclusion rests on the acquirer.

The essential difficulty with MAC clauses lies in the concept of materiality itself. What constitutes a material adverse change is inherently subjective and context-dependent. Courts across jurisdictions have struggled to develop consistent standards. In India, this difficulty is compounded by the judicial tendency to treat MAC clauses as subject to the doctrine of frustration under Section 56 of the Contract Act.

III. The Nirma Industries Standard

The most significant decision on MAC clauses in the Indian M&A context is Nirma Industries Ltd. v. SEBI (2013). Although this case arose in the context of public takeover regulations under the SEBI Takeover Code, its reasoning has shaped the judicial approach to MAC clauses in acquisition agreements more broadly.

Nirma Industries had entered into an agreement that triggered an obligation to make an open offer under the SEBI Takeover Regulations. After making the public announcement, Nirma discovered that the promoters of the target company had engaged in financial misrepresentation and fraud involving embezzlement of funds exceeding 350 crore rupees. Nirma applied to SEBI for withdrawal of the open offer, arguing that these circumstances constituted a material adverse change justifying withdrawal.

The Supreme Court rejected Nirma's application. Interpreting Regulation 27 of the 1997 Takeover Regulations, the Court held that withdrawal could be permitted only under conditions of impossibility of performance. The two expressly specified grounds in the regulation related to legal impossibility (refusal of statutory approval) and natural impossibility (death of the sole acquirer). Applying the canon of ejusdem generis, the Court concluded that any other ground for withdrawal must also fall within the same genus of impossibility.

Despite Nirma's arguments that financial misrepresentation and fraud materially affected the transaction, the Court held that withdrawal was justified only in cases of impossibility. The fact that the target company's financial health had deteriorated, even due to fraud, did not meet this standard. The Court observed that the regulatory framework for takeovers is designed to ensure certainty and finality, and that permitting withdrawal on grounds of material adverse change would undermine this objective.

This decision established that the threshold for invoking a MAC clause in the context of M&A transactions is effectively the same as the threshold for frustration under Section 56. Mere material adverse change, however substantial, is insufficient. What is required is impossibility. The rationale is rooted in the need for transactional certainty: if acquirers could walk away whenever the target's prospects dimmed, few deals would close with confidence.

IV. Subsequent Applications of the Impossibility Standard

The reasoning in Nirma Industries has been extended to cases involving financial distress and regulatory delays. In SEBI v. Akshya Infrastructure Pvt. Ltd. (2014), the Supreme Court held that an unjustifiable delay by SEBI in approving a takeover offer, which made the offer unfavourable for the acquirer, was insufficient to invoke frustration. The Court stated that economic difficulty does not come under the impossibility threshold because market fluctuations and financial strain have not been recognised as grounds for frustration of a contract.

Similarly, in Pramod Jain v. SEBI (2016), the Court held that a delay of two years by SEBI in approving an open offer did not justify withdrawal, even though the target company's financial health had deteriorated during those two years. The deterioration of financial condition, without more, did not render performance impossible. The Court emphasised that the regulatory framework prioritises certainty for target shareholders over the commercial interests of acquirers.

The SEBI order in the matter of Jyoti Private Limited (2016) is particularly instructive for understanding how this standard applies to insolvency-related events. In that case, the target company was registered as a sick industrial company with the Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction. The BIFR had ordered that status quo be maintained in terms of control of the company. The acquirers sought to withdraw their open offer, arguing that no change in control was feasible due to the pending BIFR proceedings.

SEBI rejected the request for withdrawal. Applying the reasoning in Nirma Industries, SEBI held that the completion of the open offer process was not impossible. The fact that change of control might require BIFR approval did not render the offer incapable of performance. The acquirers could still complete the transaction, albeit subject to regulatory approvals. This reasoning would apply equally to insolvency proceedings under the IBC. The existence of a moratorium may complicate the transaction, but it does not make it impossible.

VII. Conclusion

The Indian judicial approach to MAC clauses is characterised by a consistent refusal to broaden the scope of what constitutes a material adverse change. By aligning the interpretation of MAC clauses with the doctrine of frustration under Section 56 of the Contract Act, courts have set a threshold of impossibility that is difficult to satisfy. Financial distress, even when severe, does not meet this standard unless it renders performance objectively impossible.

The interaction between MAC clauses and the IBC moratorium remains largely untested. The reasoning in Nirma Industries and Jyoti Private Limited suggests that the moratorium, while disruptive, does not make performance impossible and therefore would not justify invocation of a MAC clause. The acquirer can still complete the transaction subject to the approval of the Committee of Creditors and the adjudicating authority. However, this question awaits definitive judicial clarification.

For transactional lawyers, the lesson is clear. General MAC clauses provide limited practical protection. Parties seeking to allocate the risk of financial distress must do so through carefully drafted, specific conditions tailored to the particular risks of the transaction. Objective triggers, explicit references to insolvency proceedings, and robust representations and warranties coupled with indemnities offer more reliable protection than reliance on the general language of material adverse change. The boilerplate MAC clause, in the Indian context, offers little more than false comfort.
