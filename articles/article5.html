Temporary Impossibility and Suspension of Performance under Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act

February 2026

I. INTRODUCTION

The COVID-19 pandemic precipitated a wave of contractual disputes that forced Indian courts to revisit the boundaries of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The doctrine of frustration, historically confined to situations of physical or legal impossibility, came under renewed scrutiny as parties sought to invoke force majeure clauses or the doctrine of frustration to excuse performance rendered economically burdensome by the pandemic.

Lockdowns, supply chain disruptions, and labour shortages transformed once-profitable contracts into loss-making obligations, prompting litigants to ask a fundamental question: does temporary impossibility suspend contractual obligations, or does it permanently discharge the contract under Section 56?

This article examines how Indian courts have addressed temporary impossibility arising from pandemic-related restrictions. It analyses recent High Court rulings concerning infrastructure concessions, commercial leases, and supply arrangements to determine whether temporary impediments to performance can trigger the frustration doctrine.

The article argues that Indian courts have consistently resisted treating temporary impossibility as frustration, instead favouring suspension of performance where contractual obligations remain capable of fulfilment once the impediment ceases.

II. THE STATUTORY FRAMEWORK: SECTION 56 OF THE INDIAN CONTRACT ACT

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, embodies the doctrine of frustration in Indian law.

“An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful.”

The Supreme Court’s decision in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954) remains the leading authority on the interpretation of Section 56.

III. THE PRE-PANDEMIC POSITION: A NARROW DOCTRINE

Prior to the pandemic, Indian courts consistently adopted a narrow approach to frustration. The Supreme Court reaffirmed this position in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (2017).

Similarly, in Rajasthan Rajya Vidyut Prasaran Nigam Ltd. v. DCM Shriram Consolidated Ltd. (2018), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim based on increased coal prices.

IV. PANDEMIC-ERA LITIGATION: TESTING THE DOCTRINE

Force Majeure Clauses

In Standard Retail Pvt. Ltd. v. G.S. Global Corp. (2020), the Bombay High Court considered a commodities supply contract affected by the nationwide lockdown.

Section 56 Claims

In MEP Infrastructure Developers Ltd. v. South Delhi Municipal Corporation (2020), the Delhi High Court addressed a toll concession affected by lockdown restrictions.

Similarly, in Ramanand v. Dr. N.R. (2020), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim raised by tenants seeking rent relief during the lockdown.

The Halliburton Decision

In Halliburton Offshore Services Inc. v. Vedanta Ltd. (2020), the Delhi High Court granted interim relief restraining invocation of a bank guarantee.

V. DOCTRINAL ANALYSIS

Courts continue to apply the “total destruction” test articulated in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission. Temporary impediments rarely satisfy this threshold.

Indian courts have consistently rejected economic hardship as frustration.

Temporary impossibility is typically treated as suspension of performance rather than termination of contractual obligations.

VI. COMPARATIVE PERSPECTIVES

In Canary Wharf (BP4) T1 Ltd. v. European Medicines Agency (2019), the English High Court rejected a frustration claim related to Brexit disruption.

French law recognises the doctrine of imprévision permitting judicial revision where circumstances fundamentally change.

VII. IMPLICATIONS FOR COMMERCIAL CONTRACTING

Section 56 remains a narrow doctrine. Contractual risk allocation through force majeure clauses remains the primary safeguard against extraordinary disruption.

VIII. CONCLUSION

The pandemic has tested the doctrine of frustration but has not fundamentally altered its contours under Indian law.

Temporary impossibility generally suspends performance rather than terminating contracts.
