Temporary Impossibility and Suspension of Performance under Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act

February 2026

The COVID-19 pandemic precipitated a wave of contractual disputes that forced Indian courts to revisit the boundaries of Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The doctrine of frustration, historically confined to situations of physical or legal impossibility, came under renewed scrutiny as parties sought to invoke force majeure clauses or the doctrine of frustration to excuse performance rendered economically burdensome by the pandemic. Lockdowns, supply chain disruptions, and labour shortages transformed once-profitable contracts into loss-making obligations, prompting litigants to ask a fundamental question: does temporary impossibility suspend contractual obligations, or does it permanently discharge the contract under Section 56?

This article examines how Indian courts have addressed temporary impossibility arising from pandemic-related restrictions. It analyses recent High Court rulings concerning infrastructure concessions, commercial leases, and supply arrangements to determine whether temporary impediments to performance can trigger the frustration doctrine. The article argues that Indian courts have consistently resisted treating temporary impossibility as frustration, instead favouring suspension of performance where contractual obligations remain capable of fulfilment once the impediment ceases.

II. The Statutory Framework: Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act

Section 56 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 embodies the doctrine of frustration in Indian law. The provision states:

“An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void. A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful.”

The Supreme Court’s decision in Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954) remains the leading authority on the interpretation of Section 56. The Court clarified that impossibility under the provision does not refer only to literal or physical impossibility.

III. The Pre-Pandemic Position: A Narrow Doctrine

Prior to the pandemic, Indian courts consistently adopted a narrow approach to frustration. The Supreme Court reaffirmed this position in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (2017), holding that Section 56 applies only when the contractual foundation has been completely destroyed and not merely when performance has become more onerous.

Similarly, in Rajasthan Rajya Vidyut Prasaran Nigam Ltd. v. DCM Shriram Consolidated Ltd. (2018), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim based on increased coal prices.

IV. Pandemic-Era Litigation

Where contracts contained force majeure clauses, courts generally enforced those clauses according to their terms. In Standard Retail Pvt. Ltd. v. G.S. Global Corp. (2020), the Bombay High Court considered a commodities supply contract affected by the nationwide lockdown.

In MEP Infrastructure Developers Ltd. v. South Delhi Municipal Corporation (2020), the Delhi High Court addressed a toll concession affected by lockdown restrictions.

Similarly, in Ramanand v. Dr. N.R. (2020), the Delhi High Court rejected a frustration claim raised by tenants seeking rent relief during the lockdown.

In Halliburton Offshore Services Inc. v. Vedanta Ltd. (2020), the Delhi High Court granted interim relief restraining invocation of a bank guarantee.

V. Doctrinal Analysis

Courts continue to apply the “total destruction” test articulated in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission. Temporary impediments rarely satisfy this threshold.

Indian courts have consistently rejected economic hardship as frustration. Temporary impossibility is typically treated as suspension of performance rather than termination of contractual obligations.

VI. Comparative Perspectives

In Canary Wharf (BP4) T1 Ltd. v. European Medicines Agency (2019), the English High Court rejected a frustration claim related to Brexit-related disruption.

French law recognises the doctrine of imprévision, permitting judicial revision where circumstances fundamentally change.

VII. Conclusion

The pandemic has tested the resilience of the frustration doctrine under Indian law but has not fundamentally altered its contours. Courts continue to treat Section 56 as a narrow exception to the principle that contracts must be performed.

Temporary impossibility has generally been treated as a basis for suspending performance rather than terminating contractual obligations. The “total destruction” test articulated in Energy Watchdog v. Central Electricity Regulatory Commission remains the governing standard.
