Shareholder Agreements in Early-Stage Startups: Part 1 – Governance and Founder Rights

July 2025

I. Introduction

A shareholder agreement is among the most important documents an early-stage startup will ever execute. It governs the relationship between founders and investors, allocates decision-making authority, and provides a framework for the company's growth. Unlike the company's constitutional documents, which are publicly filed and must comply strictly with the Companies Act, 2013, a shareholder agreement is a private contract that allows parties to tailor their rights and obligations to the specific needs of their venture.

This article, the first in a two-part series, examines the governance and founder-related provisions typically negotiated in early-stage funding rounds in India. It focuses on board composition, decision-making rights, founder vesting, leaver provisions, and intellectual property. Understanding these provisions is essential for founders who want to maintain control over their company while giving investors the protections they need. For investors, these provisions establish the framework within which their investment will be managed and protected.

II. The Legal Framework

Before examining specific rights, it is important to understand how shareholder agreements interact with a company's other foundational documents. The legal architecture of an Indian company consists of several layers, each serving a distinct purpose.

The Memorandum of Association is the company's charter, defining its name, registered office, objects, and capital structure. It establishes the boundaries within which the company may operate. Any activity outside these boundaries is ultra vires and void. The Memorandum can only be amended with shareholder approval and regulatory filing.

The Articles of Association are the company's internal rulebook, registered with the Registrar of Companies and publicly available. They govern the relationship between the company and its members and contain provisions on share capital, board meetings, shareholder meetings, and the rights attaching to different classes of shares. Any person who becomes a shareholder is bound by the Articles, whether or not they have read them. The Articles have statutory force under the Companies Act and prevail over inconsistent contractual provisions in certain circumstances.

A shareholder agreement is a private contract between some or all shareholders and typically the company itself. It contains detailed provisions on governance, founder restrictions, investor protections, and exit mechanisms that go beyond what is included in the Articles. The shareholder agreement is not publicly filed and can therefore include commercially sensitive arrangements that the parties prefer to keep confidential.

The relationship between these documents requires careful consideration. Where the Articles and the shareholder agreement conflict, the Articles generally prevail as a matter of company law because they have statutory status and bind all shareholders. However, the parties can agree in the shareholder agreement to amend the Articles to reflect their arrangement, and they can include provisions that are not inconsistent with the Articles. In practice, key provisions are often included in both documents. Rights that need to bind all shareholders, including future shareholders, are placed in the Articles. More detailed arrangements and rights that are primarily contractual are placed in the shareholder agreement. Making the company a party to the shareholder agreement ensures that it is bound to perform its obligations, such as issuing shares or providing information.

The Companies Act, 2013, recognises that shareholders may enter into agreements regarding the transfer of securities. Section 58(2) provides that while shares in a public company are freely transferable, any contract or arrangement between persons in respect of transfer of securities is enforceable as a contract. This statutory recognition provides the foundation for many of the provisions discussed in this article, including right of first refusal, tag-along rights, and drag-along rights.

III. Board Composition and Decision-Making

The board of directors is responsible for the company's strategic direction and major decisions. How the board is composed and how it makes decisions are critical governance questions that shape the balance of power between founders and investors.

In early-stage startups, the board typically consists of founder directors and investor nominees. Several models of board composition are possible, each with different implications for control. A founder-majority board, where founders hold more than half the seats, allows founders to control decisions without investor consent. This structure is common in very early stages where investors have limited involvement. An equal board, with equal numbers of founder and investor directors, requires consensus for decisions and often includes an independent director who can break ties. An investor-majority board gives investors control over all board decisions and is typically reserved for later stages where investors have committed substantial capital or where the company is underperforming.

A typical structure for a seed or Series A round might have three to five directors. This could include two founder directors, one investor nominee from the lead investor, an independent director sometimes added later for expertise and balance, and a seat reserved for future investors to fill in subsequent rounds. The number of directors each investor may appoint usually corresponds to the size of their investment. A lead investor investing a significant amount may negotiate for one board seat, while smaller investors may have only observer rights, allowing them to attend board meetings and receive board materials but not vote.

From a drafting perspective, the shareholder agreement should specify several elements with precision. The total number of directors and the composition of the board should be clearly stated, including how many each party may appoint and the process for appointing and removing directors. The agreement should address whether investor directors can be removed without investor consent, which is typically not permitted. The term of director appointments, usually until the next annual general meeting, should be specified, along with provisions for reappointment. The circumstances in which the board composition can be changed, such as in subsequent funding rounds, should also be addressed.

It is worth noting that investor directors owe fiduciary duties to the company, not to the investor who appointed them. This is an important protection for the company. An investor director must act in the best interests of the company, even if that conflicts with the interests of the appointing investor. This duty cannot be overridden by contract.

The shareholder agreement should also address how board meetings are conducted in practice. The frequency of meetings is typically quarterly, though more frequent meetings may be required in early stages or during periods of significant activity. Notice requirements specify how many days in advance meetings must be called, with seven to fourteen days being common. The agenda for meetings should be circulated with the notice, and provisions for adding items to the agenda should be included.

Quorum requirements deserve particular attention. Quorum is the minimum number of directors who must be present for a meeting to validly transact business. If the quorum requires at least one investor director, then no board meeting can proceed without an investor representative. This effectively gives investors veto power over all board decisions, even those that are not formally reserved matters. Founders should consider whether this level of control is appropriate for their stage of growth. Alternative formulations might require a founder director and an investor director for certain decisions, or provide that if an investor director is not present, decisions requiring their consent can be made with a higher vote threshold among remaining directors.

The shareholder agreement should specify whether directors may participate by video conference, which is now standard practice, and how decisions are recorded through minutes that must be approved at the next meeting. Provisions for passing resolutions by circulation without a meeting may also be included, typically requiring unanimous consent of all directors.

The role of chairperson is often addressed in the agreement. The chairperson sets the agenda, chairs meetings, and may have a casting vote in case of a tie. In many startups, a founder serves as chairperson, but investors may negotiate for this role in certain circumstances, particularly if they have provided substantial funding or if the company is underperforming. Some agreements provide for rotating chairpersonship or for the chairperson to be an independent director.

Observer rights are often granted to investors who do not have board seats. Observers can attend board meetings, receive board materials, and participate in discussions, but they cannot vote. This enables investors to stay informed without formal decision-making authority. The scope of observer rights can vary. Some observers have full access to all board materials and meetings. Others may be excluded from certain sessions, such as those dealing with sensitive matters like acquisitions or executive compensation. The agreement should specify what observers are entitled to receive and any limitations on their participation.

Committees of the board may also be addressed. Audit committees, compensation committees, and nomination committees are common in later-stage companies. The shareholder agreement may specify the composition of these committees and which parties have the right to appoint members. This allows for more detailed oversight of specific functions without involving the full board in every decision.

IV. Reserved Matters

Reserved matters, also known as affirmative vote or veto rights, are decisions that cannot be taken without investor consent. These provisions give investors a say in fundamental decisions even if they are minority shareholders. The scope of reserved matters is one of the most heavily negotiated areas in any shareholder agreement.

The rationale for reserved matters is straightforward. Investors commit capital based on a certain understanding of the business and its prospects. They need protection against decisions that could fundamentally alter the nature of the business, dilute their economic interest, or change the risk profile of their investment. Reserved matters ensure that investors have a voice in these fundamental decisions.

Reserved matters typically fall into several categories. Structural changes that could fundamentally alter the company include amending the memorandum or articles of association, altering the company's share capital through increase or reduction, approving mergers, acquisitions, or demergers, disposing of significant assets or the whole undertaking, commencing insolvency proceedings, and winding up the company voluntarily. These decisions change the legal or capital structure of the company and directly affect investor rights.

Financial matters that affect the company's financial position include creating any charge or encumbrance over the company's assets, borrowing money above a specified threshold, guaranteeing obligations of third parties, approving the annual business plan and budget, and making any material change in accounting policies. These provisions ensure that investors are aware of and can influence significant financial commitments.

Management matters that affect who runs the company include appointing or removing the chief executive officer, chief financial officer, and other key management personnel, determining executive remuneration including salary, bonuses, and equity grants, entering into related party transactions with founders or their affiliates, and changing the company's business or line of activity. These provisions give investors oversight of management and protection against self-dealing.

Shareholder matters that affect ownership and returns include issuing new shares or securities including convertible instruments, declaring or paying dividends, buying back shares, and creating any new share class or series. These decisions affect the ownership structure and the timing and amount of returns to shareholders.

The threshold for investor consent can be structured in various ways. Some matters may require consent of all investors, which gives each investor an individual veto. This is the strongest protection but can be difficult to obtain in practice. Other matters may require consent of a specified percentage, such as a majority or seventy-five per cent of investors. This allows decisions to proceed even if some investors object, while still providing meaningful investor protection. Some matters may have different thresholds, with more fundamental changes requiring higher consent levels.

The extent of reserved matters is a key negotiation point. Investors want broad protections to ensure they cannot be overridden on matters that affect their investment. Founders want to avoid being paralysed by having to seek consent for routine decisions. A practical approach is to set monetary thresholds for certain matters, which balances protection with operational flexibility.

For example, the sale of assets might be structured with tiers. Asset sales below a specified amount, perhaps ten lakh rupees, might require no consent and be within management's authority. Asset sales between ten lakh rupees and fifty lakh rupees might require board approval, giving directors oversight. Asset sales above fifty lakh rupees might require investor consent, ensuring that significant transactions receive investor input. This approach gives investors protection for significant transactions while allowing management to operate efficiently for smaller matters.

Some veto rights may be time-limited or subject to change based on company performance. For example, veto rights over the appointment of the chief executive officer might expire once the company reaches a certain revenue threshold, recognising that investors may be less concerned about day-to-day management as the company matures. Similarly, veto rights over the annual budget might convert to information rights after a period of consistent performance, allowing management greater autonomy.

The Competition Commission of India has provided guidance on when veto rights confer control requiring merger notification. Vetoes over business plans, senior management appointments, or operational decisions are likely to be viewed as conferring control, as they allow the investor to influence strategic decisions. Vetoes protecting purely economic interests, such as anti-dilution rights or restrictions on share issuances, are generally seen as mere investor protection and do not confer control. This distinction matters for compliance with competition law and should be considered when structuring reserved matters.

The drafting of reserved matters requires precision. Each matter should be clearly defined to avoid ambiguity about what requires consent. For example, a veto over "material contracts" requires definition of what constitutes material, whether by value, duration, or strategic importance. A veto over "changes to the business" requires clarity about what constitutes a change, whether entering new markets, launching new products, or discontinuing existing ones. Clear definitions prevent disputes about whether consent was required for a particular decision.

V. Founder Vesting

Vesting provisions ensure that founders earn their equity over time. This aligns incentives and protects the company if a founder leaves early. Vesting is one of the most important provisions for investors and one of the most significant restrictions on founders' ownership.

The rationale for vesting is straightforward. When investors commit capital, they are betting on the team. They want assurance that the founders will remain with the company and continue contributing. Vesting ensures that if a founder leaves early, they do not walk away with a large equity stake that they have not earned through continued contribution. This protects the remaining founders and investors from being diluted by someone who is no longer contributing.

Several types of vesting arrangements are possible. Time-based vesting is the most common, where shares vest based on the passage of time. Milestone-based vesting ties vesting to achievement of specific objectives, such as product launches or revenue targets. Hybrid approaches combine both, with some shares vesting over time and others upon achieving milestones. In early-stage startups, time-based vesting is standard because it is simple and predictable.

A typical vesting schedule in India is four years with a one-year cliff. This structure works as follows. If a founder leaves within the first year, they receive no equity at all. This is the cliff period. After one year, twenty-five per cent of their shares vest immediately, representing one year of the four-year period. The remaining seventy-five per cent vest monthly or quarterly over the next three years. By the end of the fourth year, all shares are fully vested.

For example, if a founder has one thousand shares with four-year vesting and a one-year cliff, no shares vest during the first year. At the end of month twelve, two hundred and fifty shares vest. Over the remaining thirty-six months, approximately fifteen to sixteen shares vest each month if vesting is monthly, or forty-five to forty-six shares vest each quarter if vesting is quarterly. The specific mechanics should be clearly defined in the agreement.

Variations on this schedule are possible. Some companies use three-year vesting with a one-year cliff, which accelerates the vesting period. Others use five-year vesting for longer-term alignment. Some agreements provide for immediate vesting of a portion of shares, often called an initial grant, with the remainder vesting over time. This recognises that founders have already contributed value before the investment and should have some equity that is not subject to vesting.

Accelerated vesting provisions allow shares to vest faster upon certain events. Several types of acceleration are common. Single-trigger acceleration provides that shares vest immediately upon a specified event, such as a change of control of the company. This protects founders if the company is acquired and their role may change or be eliminated. Double-trigger acceleration requires two events: a change of control and subsequent termination without cause. This is more investor-friendly because it encourages founders to remain with the company post-acquisition. If the acquirer wants the founders to stay, they continue working and their shares continue vesting normally. If the acquirer terminates them without cause, their shares accelerate.

The scope of acceleration should be carefully considered. Full acceleration vests all unvested shares immediately. Partial acceleration vests only a portion, such as the shares that would have vested in the next twelve months. The choice depends on the negotiation and the circumstances. Founders naturally seek broader acceleration. Investors prefer narrower acceleration to encourage retention.

Accelerated vesting may also be triggered by termination without cause or constructive termination, such as a material reduction in responsibilities or relocation without consent. These provisions protect founders from being forced out without cause and losing their unvested equity. The definition of constructive termination should be carefully drafted to avoid ambiguity.

Key drafting considerations for vesting include several elements. The vesting period and cliff period must be clearly specified, with precise start and end dates. The treatment of unvested shares upon departure must be addressed, with unvested shares typically being cancelled or repurchased by the company at nominal value. The treatment of vested shares upon departure depends on leaver classification, discussed in the next section. The interaction with option pools and employee equity should be considered, ensuring that founder vesting is consistent with the company's broader equity incentive plans.

Founder vesting is often a point of negotiation, particularly with experienced founders who have built successful companies before. Some founders argue that they should not be subject to vesting because they have already proven themselves. Investors typically respond that vesting is standard practice and necessary to align interests. A compromise may involve a shorter vesting period or a larger initial grant of vested shares.

Ideally, founders agree on vesting among themselves before approaching investors. A founders' agreement can establish vesting terms that apply to all founders equally, preventing one founder from being treated more favourably than others. This demonstrates to investors that the founder group is aligned and has already addressed these issues. It also prevents renegotiation at the time of investment, when founders may be in a weaker bargaining position.

VI. Leaver Provisions

What happens when a founder's employment ends is addressed by leaver provisions. These provisions determine the consequences of departure and are closely linked to vesting. The classification of a departing founder as a good leaver or bad leaver has significant economic consequences.

Good leavers are those whose departure occurs through no fault of their own. The definition of good leaver typically includes death or permanent disability, which are clearly beyond the founder's control. Retirement with board consent may also be included, though retirement is uncommon in early-stage startups. Termination by the company without cause is typically treated as good leaver, meaning the company has decided to end the relationship for reasons not amounting to cause. Mutual agreement between the founder and the company may also be included, though this requires case-by-case assessment.

A good leaver is typically entitled to retain all vested shares. The rationale is that these shares have been earned through past service and should not be forfeited. Some agreements also require the company to purchase the vested shares at fair value, which provides liquidity to the departing founder and removes them from the share register. Fair value is typically determined by a recent valuation, such as the valuation in the most recent funding round, or by an independent appraisal. Unvested shares are forfeited and cancelled, as they have not been earned.

Bad leavers are those terminated for cause. Cause is typically defined to include serious misconduct that justifies forfeiture of economic benefits. Common cause definitions include fraud, dishonesty, or gross negligence in the performance of duties. Conviction of a criminal offence that impacts the company or the founder's ability to serve is also typically included. Material breach of the shareholder agreement, employment contract, or fiduciary duties after notice and failure to cure may be included. Persistent failure to perform duties after notice and opportunity to improve may also be included. Competition with the company or solicitation of employees or customers during employment or after departure is often specified.

A bad leaver is typically required to sell their shares back to the company or other shareholders at a discounted price. The most common pricing is the lower of original purchase price and fair market value, often resulting in the founder receiving only what they paid for the shares. In some cases, particularly for serious misconduct like fraud, the shares may be forfeited entirely without compensation. Unvested shares are forfeited in all cases.

The definition of cause is critical and heavily negotiated. Founders should seek objective standards that are clear and measurable. Fraud and criminal conviction are objective. Gross negligence and material breach are more subjective and may require further definition. Where possible, founders should seek a cure period for breaches that can be remedied. For example, a breach of contract might be curable if the founder corrects it within thirty days of receiving notice specifying the breach. This prevents minor or technical breaches from triggering bad leaver treatment.

Investors typically resist requiring a court determination of cause given the timelines involved. A court proceeding could take years, during which time the founder might remain on the share register and continue to exercise rights. A common compromise is to require a determination by the board, excluding the founder in question. This allows for a timely decision while providing some procedural fairness. Some agreements provide for arbitration of cause disputes, which is faster than court proceedings.

The distinction between vested and unvested shares matters significantly. Vested shares belong to the founder and cannot be taken away without compensation, even for bad leavers. The question is what compensation is appropriate. Unvested shares are forfeited regardless of leaver status, as they have not been earned.

For good leavers, the question is whether they must sell their vested shares or can retain them. Many agreements require all departing founders to sell their vested shares to ensure that former founders do not remain on the share register indefinitely. Former shareholders can complicate future decisions requiring shareholder approval and may have ongoing information rights. Requiring sale provides a clean break. The price for such sales is typically fair market value, providing the departing founder with liquidity.

For bad leavers, the sale of vested shares is mandatory, and the price is heavily discounted to reflect the circumstances of departure. This serves as a deterrent and ensures that founders who engage in misconduct do not profit from their shares.

Leaver provisions should also address the mechanics of the share transfer. The departing founder must execute share transfer forms and deliver share certificates. If they fail to do so, the agreement should provide for the company to effect the transfer through a power of attorney or other mechanism. The consideration should be paid promptly upon transfer.

VII. Intellectual Property

A recurring issue in early-stage ventures is disputes over intellectual property ownership when founders separate. Clear intellectual property provisions are essential to ensure that the company owns the assets it needs to operate and grow. Investors will require clean intellectual property ownership as a condition of investment, and disputes over intellectual property can derail future funding rounds or acquisitions.

The intellectual property provisions in a shareholder agreement serve several purposes. They confirm that all intellectual property created by founders in connection with the business belongs to the company. They address pre-existing intellectual property that founders bring to the company. They establish ongoing obligations to protect and perfect the company's intellectual property rights. And they provide mechanisms for resolving any disputes that may arise.

The core provision is the intellectual property assignment clause. This should include several key elements. An acknowledgment that all intellectual property created before the agreement in connection with the business is assigned to the company, ensuring that work done before incorporation or investment is captured. An obligation to assign future intellectual property created during employment or engagement, covering ongoing contributions. A requirement to execute any documents necessary to perfect the company's ownership, such as patent assignments or trademark registrations. A confirmation that the founder has no further rights in the intellectual property and will not use it outside the company's business.

The definition of intellectual property should be broad enough to cover all relevant rights. This typically includes patents, trademarks, copyrights, designs, trade secrets, know-how, and any other intellectual or industrial property rights. The definition should also cover software, source code, databases, and other technology assets that are critical to technology startups.

Founders may bring pre-existing intellectual property to the company. This background intellectual property should be identified in a schedule to the agreement, with a clear description of what it is and how it was created. The ownership of background intellectual property remains with the founder, but the founder grants the company a perpetual, royalty-free licence to use the intellectual property for the company's business. This licence should be irrevocable and transferable, meaning it survives the founder's departure and can be passed to an acquirer if the company is sold.

The scope of the licence should be carefully considered. It should cover all uses of the intellectual property in the company's business as it exists and as it may evolve. It should include the right to sublicense, allowing the company to authorise third parties such as customers or partners to use the intellectual property. It should be worldwide, as the company may operate in multiple jurisdictions. And it should be royalty-free, meaning the company does not have to pay ongoing fees to the founder.

In India, creators have moral rights in their work under the Copyright Act. These include the right to attribution, meaning the creator's name should be associated with the work, and the right to integrity, meaning the work should not be distorted or mutilated in a way that harms the creator's reputation. Moral rights cannot be assigned, but creators can waive them to the extent necessary for the company to exploit the intellectual property. The agreement should include a waiver of moral rights in favour of the company, allowing the company to use, modify, and adapt the intellectual property without restriction.

The shareholder agreement should also address confidentiality. Founders must keep confidential information about the company's business, technology, plans, and operations confidential. This obligation should survive termination of employment or engagement, continuing indefinitely. The definition of confidential information should be broad enough to cover all sensitive information but should exclude information that is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the founder.

Beyond the agreement, practical steps are essential to protect intellectual property. The company should maintain records of intellectual property creation, including dates of creation, inventors or authors, and circumstances of creation. This documentation can be critical in disputes about ownership or inventorship. The company should register patents, trademarks, and designs in its own name as soon as practicable, establishing public record of ownership. The company should ensure all employees and contractors sign appropriate intellectual property assignment agreements before starting work, capturing rights at the time of creation. The company should conduct intellectual property audits before fundraising, identifying any gaps or issues that need to be addressed.

For technology startups, source code ownership requires particular attention. The agreement should confirm that all source code developed by founders or employees is owned by the company. If third-party developers are used, the company should have written agreements assigning all rights. Open source software used in the company's products should be identified and compliance with licence terms verified.

VIII. Founder Roles and Responsibilities

Beyond vesting and intellectual property, the shareholder agreement should address the roles and responsibilities of founders. Clear definition of roles prevents disputes and ensures that everyone understands what is expected of them.

The agreement should specify each founder's title and primary responsibilities. This might include chief executive officer responsible for overall strategy and fundraising, chief technology officer responsible for product development, and chief operating officer responsible for operations and finance. These roles may evolve over time, and the agreement should provide for changes with board approval.

The time commitment expected of founders should be addressed. Full-time commitment is standard, meaning founders should devote substantially all their business time and effort to the company. Permitted activities outside the company, such as advisory roles or board seats with prior companies, should be disclosed and approved. Moonlighting or working on other ventures should generally be prohibited.

Compensation for founders should be specified. Early-stage founders often take minimal salary to conserve cash, with the expectation that their primary reward will come from equity appreciation. The agreement should establish the initial compensation and the process for reviewing and adjusting compensation over time, typically through board approval.

Founder dispute resolution mechanisms should be included. Despite best intentions, founders sometimes disagree. The agreement should provide a process for resolving disputes, such as mediation before arbitration or a deadlock-breaking mechanism. This prevents minor disagreements from escalating into major conflicts that could destroy the company.

IX. Practical Considerations

Several practical considerations emerge from the governance and founder provisions discussed above. These considerations should guide both drafting and negotiation.

Before approaching investors, founders should align among themselves. A founders' agreement addressing vesting, roles, intellectual property, and what happens if a founder leaves can prevent disputes later. Investors will expect these issues to be resolved before they invest, and having a founders' agreement demonstrates that the founder group is cohesive and prepared.

Board composition affects how decisions are made and how the company feels to those involved. Founders should consider not just who has voting power, but also how the board will function in practice. A board that is too large can be unwieldy, with too many voices and slow decision-making. A board with too many investor directors may feel adversarial, with founders constantly defending their decisions. Finding the right balance for the company's stage and culture is important.

When negotiating reserved matters, focus on thresholds that make sense for the business. A veto over all asset sales might be too broad and could prevent the company from making routine disposals of old equipment. A veto over sales above a reasonable amount, such as five per cent of the company's assets or a specified rupee value, provides protection without paralysing management. Similarly, borrowing limits should be set at levels that allow the company to operate without constant consent while protecting against excessive debt.

The leaver provisions should be fair to both sides. Founders should not be treated as bad leavers for minor infractions or performance issues that are not their fault. Investors need protection against serious misconduct that could harm the company. A balanced approach with clear definitions, objective standards, and cure periods where appropriate serves both interests. The definition of cause should be carefully negotiated to avoid ambiguity.

Intellectual property provisions should be comprehensive and supported by practical steps. The agreement alone is not enough. The company must maintain records, register rights, and ensure all contributors sign appropriate agreements. Investors will conduct intellectual property due diligence and expect to see clean chains of title.

All of these provisions should be documented in the shareholder agreement and, where appropriate, in the articles of association. Key governance provisions in the articles bind all shareholders, including future ones who were not party to the original agreement. The articles should be reviewed and updated each time the shareholder agreement is amended to ensure consistency.

X. Conclusion

The governance and founder provisions in a shareholder agreement establish the framework for how the company will be run. Board composition determines who makes decisions and how those decisions are made. Reserved matters define which fundamental decisions require investor consent, balancing protection with operational flexibility. Founder vesting aligns incentives with long-term value creation, ensuring that founders earn their equity through continued contribution. Leaver provisions address what happens when founders depart, protecting the company and remaining shareholders. Intellectual property provisions ensure the company owns the assets it needs to grow and succeed.

For founders, understanding these provisions is essential. They define the founders' role in the company, their economic interest, and what happens if things go wrong. They establish the boundaries within which founders can operate and the protections they have against arbitrary action. They determine how much control founders will have and under what circumstances that control can be diluted.

For investors, these provisions provide the basic protections needed to invest with confidence. They ensure that investors have a voice in fundamental decisions, that founder incentives are aligned with long-term value creation, and that the company owns its critical intellectual property assets. They establish the framework for the relationship with founders that will last throughout the life of the investment.
