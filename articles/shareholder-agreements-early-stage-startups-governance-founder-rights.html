Shareholder Agreements in Early-Stage Startups: Part 1 – Governance and Founder Rights

By A
August 2025

I. Introduction

A shareholder agreement is among the most important documents an early-stage startup will ever execute. It governs the relationship between founders and investors, allocates decision-making authority, and provides a framework for the company's growth. Unlike the company's constitutional documents, which are publicly filed and must comply strictly with the Companies Act, 2013, a shareholder agreement is a private contract that allows parties to tailor their rights and obligations to the specific needs of their venture.

This article, the first in a two-part series, examines the governance and founder-related provisions typically negotiated in early-stage funding rounds in India. It focuses on board composition, decision-making rights, founder vesting, leaver provisions, and intellectual property. Understanding these provisions is essential for founders who want to maintain control over their company while giving investors the protections they need.

II. The Legal Framework

Before examining specific rights, it is important to understand how shareholder agreements interact with a company's other foundational documents. The Articles of Association are the company's constitutional document, registered with the Registrar of Companies and publicly available. They govern the relationship between the company and its members and contain basic provisions on share capital, board meetings, and shareholder meetings. Any person who becomes a shareholder is bound by the Articles.

A shareholder agreement is a private contract between some or all shareholders and typically the company itself. It contains detailed provisions on governance, founder restrictions, investor protections, and exit mechanisms that go beyond what is included in the Articles. In practice, key provisions are often included in both documents. Rights that need to bind all shareholders, including future shareholders, are placed in the Articles. More detailed arrangements and rights that are primarily contractual are placed in the shareholder agreement. Making the company a party to the shareholder agreement ensures that it is bound to perform its obligations, such as issuing shares or providing information.

The Companies Act, 2013, recognises that shareholders may enter into agreements regarding the transfer of securities. Section 58(2) provides that while shares in a public company are freely transferable, any contract or arrangement between persons in respect of transfer of securities is enforceable as a contract. This statutory recognition provides the foundation for many of the provisions discussed in this article.

III. Board Composition and Decision-Making

The board of directors is responsible for the company's strategic direction and major decisions. How the board is composed and how it makes decisions are critical governance questions. In early-stage startups, the board typically consists of founder directors and investor nominees.

A typical structure might have three to five directors, including two founder directors, one investor nominee, an independent director sometimes added later, and a seat reserved for future investors.

The number of directors each investor may appoint usually corresponds to the size of their investment. A lead investor investing a significant amount may negotiate for one board seat, while smaller investors may have only observer rights.

IV. Reserved Matters

Reserved matters, also known as affirmative vote or veto rights, are decisions that cannot be taken without investor consent. These provisions give investors a say in fundamental decisions even if they are minority shareholders.

Structural changes commonly covered include amending the memorandum or articles of association, altering the company's share capital, approving mergers, acquisitions, or disposals of significant assets, commencing insolvency proceedings, and winding up the company.

V. Founder Vesting

Vesting provisions ensure that founders earn their equity over time. This aligns incentives and protects the company if a founder leaves early. A typical vesting schedule in India is four years with a one-year cliff.

This means that if a founder leaves within the first year, they receive no equity. After one year, twenty-five per cent of their shares vest immediately.

VI. Leaver Provisions

What happens when a founder's employment ends is addressed by leaver provisions. The consequences depend on whether the founder is classified as a good leaver or a bad leaver.

Good leavers typically include those whose employment ceases due to death or permanent disability, retirement with board consent, or termination by the company without cause.

VII. Intellectual Property

A recurring issue in early-stage ventures is disputes over intellectual property ownership when founders separate. Clear intellectual property provisions are essential.

The shareholder agreement should include provisions that any intellectual property developed by a founder in connection with the business is owned by the company.

VIII. Practical Considerations

Before approaching investors, founders should align among themselves. A founders' agreement addressing vesting, roles, intellectual property, and what happens if a founder leaves can prevent disputes later.

IX. Conclusion

The governance and founder provisions in a shareholder agreement establish the framework for how the company will be run. Board composition determines who makes decisions. Reserved matters define which decisions require investor consent.

For founders, understanding these provisions is essential. For investors, these provisions provide the protections needed to invest with confidence.
