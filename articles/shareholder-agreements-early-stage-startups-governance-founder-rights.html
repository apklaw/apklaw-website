Shareholder Agreements in Early-Stage Startups: Part 1 – Governance and Founder Rights

August 2025

I. Introduction

A shareholder agreement is among the most important documents an early-stage startup will ever execute. It governs the relationship between founders and investors, allocates decision-making authority, and provides a framework for the company's growth. Unlike the company's constitutional documents, which are publicly filed and must comply strictly with the Companies Act, 2013, a shareholder agreement is a private contract that allows parties to tailor their rights and obligations to the specific needs of their venture.

This article, the first in a two-part series, examines the governance and founder-related provisions typically negotiated in early-stage funding rounds in India. It focuses on board composition, decision-making rights, founder vesting, leaver provisions, and intellectual property. Understanding these provisions is essential for founders who want to maintain control over their company while giving investors the protections they need.

II. The Legal Framework

Before examining specific rights, it is important to understand how shareholder agreements interact with a company's other foundational documents. The Articles of Association are the company's constitutional document, registered with the Registrar of Companies and publicly available. They govern the relationship between the company and its members and contain basic provisions on share capital, board meetings, and shareholder meetings. Any person who becomes a shareholder is bound by the Articles.

A shareholder agreement is a private contract between some or all shareholders and typically the company itself. It contains detailed provisions on governance, founder restrictions, investor protections, and exit mechanisms that go beyond what is included in the Articles. In practice, key provisions are often included in both documents. Rights that need to bind all shareholders, including future shareholders, are placed in the Articles. More detailed arrangements and rights that are primarily contractual are placed in the shareholder agreement. Making the company a party to the shareholder agreement ensures that it is bound to perform its obligations, such as issuing shares or providing information.

The Companies Act, 2013, recognises that shareholders may enter into agreements regarding the transfer of securities. Section 58(2) provides that while shares in a public company are freely transferable, any contract or arrangement between persons in respect of transfer of securities is enforceable as a contract. This statutory recognition provides the foundation for many of the provisions discussed in this article.

III. Board Composition and Decision-Making

The board of directors is responsible for the company's strategic direction and major decisions. How the board is composed and how it makes decisions are critical governance questions. In early-stage startups, the board typically consists of founder directors and investor nominees. A typical structure might have three to five directors, including two founder directors, one investor nominee, an independent director sometimes added later, and a seat reserved for future investors.

The number of directors each investor may appoint usually corresponds to the size of their investment. A lead investor investing a significant amount may negotiate for one board seat, while smaller investors may have only observer rights, allowing them to attend board meetings but not vote.

From a drafting perspective, the shareholder agreement should specify the total number of directors, how many each party may appoint, the procedure for appointment and removal, and whether investor directors can be removed without investor consent. It is worth noting that investor directors owe fiduciary duties to the company, not to the investor who appointed them, which is an important protection for the company.

The shareholder agreement should also address how board meetings are conducted. Key provisions include the frequency of meetings, typically quarterly, notice requirements specifying how many days in advance meetings must be called, quorum often requiring at least one founder director and one investor director, whether directors may participate by video conference, and how decisions are recorded.

Quorum requirements deserve particular attention. If the quorum requires at least one investor director, then no board meeting can proceed without an investor representative, effectively giving investors veto power over all board decisions. Founders should consider whether this level of control is appropriate for their stage of growth.

The shareholder agreement may also specify who serves as chairperson of the board. The chairperson typically sets the agenda, chairs meetings, and may have a casting vote in case of a tie. In many startups, a founder serves as chairperson, but investors may negotiate for this role in certain circumstances.

Investors who do not have board seats may negotiate for observer rights, allowing them to attend board meetings, receive board materials, and participate in discussions without voting. This enables investors to stay informed without formal decision-making authority.

IV. Reserved Matters

Reserved matters, also known as affirmative vote or veto rights, are decisions that cannot be taken without investor consent. These provisions give investors a say in fundamental decisions even if they are minority shareholders.

Structural changes commonly covered include amending the memorandum or articles of association, altering the company's share capital, approving mergers, acquisitions, or disposals of significant assets, commencing insolvency proceedings, and winding up the company.

Financial matters include creating any charge or encumbrance over the company's assets, borrowing money above a specified threshold, guaranteeing obligations of third parties, and approving the annual business plan and budget.

Management matters include appointing or removing the chief executive officer and chief financial officer, determining executive remuneration, entering into related party transactions, and changing the company's business or line of activity.

Shareholder matters include issuing new shares or securities, declaring dividends, and buying back shares.

The extent of reserved matters is a key negotiation point. Investors want broad protections, while founders want to avoid being paralysed by having to seek consent for routine decisions. A practical approach is to set monetary thresholds for certain matters.

Some veto rights may be time-limited. For example, veto rights over the appointment of the chief executive officer might expire once the company reaches a certain size or stage.

The Competition Commission of India has provided guidance on when veto rights confer control requiring merger notification. Vetoes over business plans, senior management appointments, or operational decisions are likely to be viewed as conferring control, while vetoes protecting purely economic interests such as anti-dilution rights are generally seen as mere investor protection.

V. Founder Vesting

Vesting provisions ensure that founders earn their equity over time. This aligns incentives and protects the company if a founder leaves early. A typical vesting schedule in India is four years with a one-year cliff.

This means that if a founder leaves within the first year, they receive no equity. After one year, twenty-five per cent of their shares vest immediately, and the remaining seventy-five per cent vest monthly or quarterly over the next three years.

The rationale for vesting is straightforward. It aligns incentives by rewarding continued contribution and protects the company and co-founders if a founder leaves early. Without vesting, a departing founder might retain a large equity stake while contributing nothing to future growth.

Accelerated vesting provisions allow shares to vest faster upon certain events. Common triggers include a change of control such as an acquisition of the company, termination without cause, or constructive termination such as a material reduction in responsibilities.

VI. Leaver Provisions

What happens when a founder's employment ends is addressed by leaver provisions. The consequences depend on whether the founder is classified as a good leaver or a bad leaver.

Good leavers typically include those whose employment ceases due to death or permanent disability, retirement with board consent, termination by the company without cause, or mutual agreement between the founder and the company.

Bad leavers are those terminated for cause. Cause typically includes fraud, dishonesty, or gross negligence, conviction of a criminal offence, material breach of the shareholder agreement or employment contract, breach of fiduciary duty, or persistent failure to perform duties after notice.

The definition of cause is critical. Founders should seek objective standards and, where possible, a cure period for breaches that can be remedied.

The distinction between vested and unvested shares matters significantly. Vested shares belong to the founder and cannot be taken away without compensation. Unvested shares are typically forfeited upon departure.

VII. Intellectual Property

A recurring issue in early-stage ventures is disputes over intellectual property ownership when founders separate. Clear intellectual property provisions are essential.

The shareholder agreement should include clear provisions that any intellectual property developed by a founder in connection with the business is owned by the company. This typically takes the form of an intellectual property assignment clause.

Key elements include an acknowledgment that all intellectual property created before the agreement in connection with the business is assigned to the company, an obligation to assign future intellectual property created during employment or engagement, a requirement to execute any documents necessary to perfect the company's ownership, and a confirmation that the founder has no further rights in the intellectual property.

In India, creators have moral rights in their work, including the right to attribution and the right to integrity. While these rights cannot be assigned, founders can waive them to the extent necessary for the company to exploit the intellectual property.

VIII. Practical Considerations for Part 1

Several practical considerations emerge from the governance and founder provisions discussed above. Before approaching investors, founders should align among themselves.

Board composition affects how decisions are made. Founders should consider not just who has voting power, but also how the board will function in practice.

When negotiating reserved matters, founders should focus on thresholds that make sense for the business.

The leaver provisions should be fair to both sides. Founders should not be treated as bad leavers for minor infractions, while investors need protection against serious misconduct.

IX. Conclusion

The governance and founder provisions in a shareholder agreement establish the framework for how the company will be run. Board composition determines who makes decisions. Reserved matters define which decisions require investor consent. Vesting aligns founder incentives with long-term value creation. Leaver provisions address what happens when founders depart. Intellectual property provisions ensure the company owns the assets it needs to grow.

For founders, understanding these provisions is essential. They define the founders' role in the company, their economic interest, and what happens if things go wrong. For investors, these provisions provide the basic protections needed to invest with confidence.
