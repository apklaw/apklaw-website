Shareholder Agreements in Early-Stage Startups: Part 1 – Governance and Founder Rights

August 2025

I. Introduction

A shareholder agreement is among the most important documents an early-stage startup will ever execute. It governs the relationship between founders and investors, allocates decision-making authority, and provides a framework for the company's growth. Unlike the company's constitutional documents, which are publicly filed and must comply strictly with the Companies Act, 2013, a shareholder agreement is a private contract that allows parties to tailor their rights and obligations to the specific needs of their venture.

This article, the first in a two-part series, examines the governance and founder-related provisions typically negotiated in early-stage funding rounds in India. It focuses on board composition, decision-making rights, founder vesting, leaver provisions, and intellectual property. Understanding these provisions is essential for founders who want to maintain control over their company while giving investors the protections they need. For investors, these provisions establish the framework within which their investment will be managed and protected.

II. The Legal Framework

Before examining specific rights, it is important to understand how shareholder agreements interact with a company's other foundational documents. The legal architecture of an Indian company consists of several layers, each serving a distinct purpose.

The Memorandum of Association is the company's charter, defining its name, registered office, objects, and capital structure. It establishes the boundaries within which the company may operate. Any activity outside these boundaries is ultra vires and void. The Memorandum can only be amended with shareholder approval and regulatory filing.

The Articles of Association are the company's internal rulebook, registered with the Registrar of Companies and publicly available. They govern the relationship between the company and its members and contain provisions on share capital, board meetings, shareholder meetings, and the rights attaching to different classes of shares. Any person who becomes a shareholder is bound by the Articles, whether or not they have read them. The Articles have statutory force under the Companies Act and prevail over inconsistent contractual provisions in certain circumstances.

A shareholder agreement is a private contract between some or all shareholders and typically the company itself. It contains detailed provisions on governance, founder restrictions, investor protections, and exit mechanisms that go beyond what is included in the Articles. The shareholder agreement is not publicly filed and can therefore include commercially sensitive arrangements that the parties prefer to keep confidential.

The relationship between these documents requires careful consideration. Where the Articles and the shareholder agreement conflict, the Articles generally prevail as a matter of company law because they have statutory status and bind all shareholders. However, the parties can agree in the shareholder agreement to amend the Articles to reflect their arrangement, and they can include provisions that are not inconsistent with the Articles. In practice, key provisions are often included in both documents. Rights that need to bind all shareholders, including future shareholders, are placed in the Articles. More detailed arrangements and rights that are primarily contractual are placed in the shareholder agreement. Making the company a party to the shareholder agreement ensures that it is bound to perform its obligations, such as issuing shares or providing information.

The Companies Act, 2013, recognises that shareholders may enter into agreements regarding the transfer of securities. Section 58(2) provides that while shares in a public company are freely transferable, any contract or arrangement between persons in respect of transfer of securities is enforceable as a contract. This statutory recognition provides the foundation for many of the provisions discussed in this article, including right of first refusal, tag-along rights, and drag-along rights.

III. Board Composition and Decision-Making

The board of directors is responsible for the company's strategic direction and major decisions. How the board is composed and how it makes decisions are critical governance questions that shape the balance of power between founders and investors.

In early-stage startups, the board typically consists of founder directors and investor nominees. Several models of board composition are possible, each with different implications for control. A founder-majority board, where founders hold more than half the seats, allows founders to control decisions without investor consent. This structure is common in very early stages where investors have limited involvement. An equal board, with equal numbers of founder and investor directors, requires consensus for decisions and often includes an independent director who can break ties. An investor-majority board gives investors control over all board decisions and is typically reserved for later stages where investors have committed substantial capital or where the company is underperforming.

A typical structure for a seed or Series A round might have three to five directors. This could include two founder directors, one investor nominee from the lead investor, an independent director sometimes added later for expertise and balance, and a seat reserved for future investors to fill in subsequent rounds. The number of directors each investor may appoint usually corresponds to the size of their investment. A lead investor investing a significant amount may negotiate for one board seat, while smaller investors may have only observer rights.

IV. Reserved Matters

Reserved matters, also known as affirmative vote or veto rights, are decisions that cannot be taken without investor consent. These provisions give investors a say in fundamental decisions even if they are minority shareholders.

V. Founder Vesting

Vesting provisions ensure that founders earn their equity over time. This aligns incentives and protects the company if a founder leaves early.

VI. Leaver Provisions

What happens when a founder's employment ends is addressed by leaver provisions. These provisions determine the consequences of departure and are closely linked to vesting.

VII. Intellectual Property

A recurring issue in early-stage ventures is disputes over intellectual property ownership when founders separate. Clear intellectual property provisions are essential to ensure that the company owns the assets it needs to operate and grow.

VIII. Founder Roles and Responsibilities

Beyond vesting and intellectual property, the shareholder agreement should address the roles and responsibilities of founders.

IX. Practical Considerations

Several practical considerations emerge from the governance and founder provisions discussed above.

X. Conclusion

The governance and founder provisions in a shareholder agreement establish the framework for how the company will be run. Board composition determines who makes decisions and how those decisions are made. Reserved matters define which fundamental decisions require investor consent, balancing protection with operational flexibility. Founder vesting aligns incentives with long-term value creation. Leaver provisions address what happens when founders depart. Intellectual property provisions ensure the company owns the assets it needs to grow and succeed.
