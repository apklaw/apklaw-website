The Summary Suit under Order XXXVII CPC: Scope, the Leave to Defend Standard, and the Commercial Courts Overlay

2026

I. Introduction

Indian civil procedure offers, in the ordinary course, a full trial as the principal vehicle for resolving contested money claims. The plaintiff pleads, the defendant responds, issues are framed, evidence is led, and arguments are heard before judgment is delivered. This process, however thorough in design, is rarely swift in practice. Order XXXVII of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 provides a deliberate departure from this model for a defined and narrow class of monetary claims: it allows a plaintiff, armed with a written instrument or a negotiable instrument evidencing an acknowledged liability, to obtain judgment without trial unless the defendant can demonstrate, at the threshold, a defence worthy of investigation. The instrument is not intended to deny defendants a hearing; it is intended to deny them delay as a strategy.

The doctrine that governs the threshold question, namely whether a defendant is entitled to leave to defend and on what terms, has been developed over several decades by the Supreme Court and the High Courts. The foundational principles were articulated in the celebrated decision in Mechelec Engineers and Manufacturers v. Basic Equipment Corporation (1976) 4 SCC 687 and have been consistently applied and refined since. At the same time, the enactment of the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 has introduced a parallel summary mechanism under Order XIII-A of the Code, which operates alongside, and not in substitution of, Order XXXVII. The relationship between the two regimes, and the procedural choices available to a plaintiff in a commercial dispute, deserves careful analysis.

This article examines the scope of suits maintainable under Order XXXVII, the procedural pathway from institution to decree, the doctrinal framework for granting or refusing leave to defend, the powers of the court to set aside an ex parte decree under Rule 4, and the intersection of the Order XXXVII regime with the Commercial Courts Act.

II. The Statutory Framework and Classes of Suits

Order XXXVII Rule 1 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 defines the category of suits to which the summary procedure applies. The Order applies, in the first instance, to suits upon bills of exchange, hundies, and promissory notes. It applies, in addition, to suits in which the plaintiff seeks to recover a debt or a liquidated demand arising on a written contract, or on a guarantee, where the claim against the principal is in respect of a debt or liquidated demand, or on a foreign judgment. The High Courts, by their respective rules, have extended the operation of Order XXXVII to other classes of suits, and several High Courts apply the Order to suits for the recovery of money based on written agreements relating to the supply of goods or services.

Two conditions run through all permissible categories. First, the claim must be for a debt or a liquidated demand in money, whether or not with interest. A liquidated demand is one where the amount is ascertained or ascertainable with certainty, as by reference to a contract or an instrument, without reference to opinion or unresolved factual inquiry. A claim for unliquidated damages, or for compensation the assessment of which requires the court to form a judgment on competing estimates, is not maintainable under Order XXXVII. Second, and critically, the plaint itself must state that the suit is filed under Order XXXVII and that no relief other than that claimed in the plaint is being sought. Courts have dismissed plaints that seek additional equitable or declaratory relief alongside the money claim, on the ground that such plaints step outside the confined scope of the summary procedure.

The requirement that the instrument underlying the claim be a "written contract" has generated a significant body of case law on the question of what documents qualify. Unsigned invoices, acknowledgments of debt, and confirmation letters have variously been held by different High Courts to constitute a sufficient written basis for a summary suit, provided that the obligation is clear and the amount is quantifiable. The Delhi High Court, in Flick Studios Pvt. Ltd. v. Gravity Entertainment Pvt. Ltd. (2024), held that unsigned invoices may constitute a valid basis for filing a suit under Order XXXVII, reasoning that the written nature of the instrument and the certainty of the claimed amount are the operative criteria, rather than the formality of execution. The decision reflects a purposive approach to the scope of the Order consistent with the underlying policy of facilitating recovery of acknowledged commercial liabilities.

III. The Procedural Pathway from Institution to Decree

A suit under Order XXXVII is instituted by the presentation of a plaint in the prescribed manner. The court, on being satisfied that the plaint is in order, issues a specially endorsed summons requiring the defendant to enter appearance within ten days of service. The endorsement on the summons notifies the defendant that in default of appearance, the plaintiff is entitled to judgment for the sum claimed with interest and costs. The ten-day appearance window is thus a critical procedural threshold, and failure to appear within it has immediate and severe consequences: under Rule 2(3), if the defendant does not enter appearance within the time specified, the averments in the plaint are deemed admitted and the plaintiff becomes entitled to a decree forthwith for the sum claimed together with interest and costs.

If the defendant enters appearance within the prescribed time, the plaintiff must serve on the defendant a summons for judgment in the prescribed form. From the date of service of the summons for judgment, the defendant has a further ten days within which to apply for leave to defend the suit, supported by an affidavit disclosing the facts on which the proposed defence is based. If the defendant does not apply for leave within this period, or if the application for leave is made but refused, the plaintiff is again entitled to judgment forthwith. The procedure is thus structured around two consecutive gatekeeping steps: first, the obligation to appear; and second, the obligation to demonstrate, at the affidavit stage, that the proposed defence is worthy of trial.

The plaint in a summary suit must be accompanied by all documents that the plaintiff relies upon, including the instrument evidencing the debt and any notice of demand served on the defendant. The court fee is payable on the amount claimed, and the plaint must be verified in the manner prescribed under Order VI Rule 15 of the Code. In Karnataka, summary suits falling within the pecuniary jurisdiction of the City Civil Court, Bengaluru are maintainable before that court in respect of suits not exceeding two crore rupees in value. Suits exceeding that threshold, being ordinary original civil jurisdiction matters, lie before the High Court of Karnataka or, where they qualify as commercial disputes of a specified value under the Commercial Courts Act, 2015, before the designated Commercial Court in Bengaluru.

IV. The Leave to Defend Standard: The Mechelec Principles

The central doctrinal question in any contested summary suit is whether, and on what terms, the defendant is entitled to leave to defend. The principles governing this question were definitively stated by the Supreme Court in Mechelec Engineers and Manufacturers v. Basic Equipment Corporation (1976) 4 SCC 687, a decision that has remained the locus classicus on the subject for nearly five decades and continues to be applied as the governing authority by courts across the country.

The facts in Mechelec arose from a summary suit instituted on the basis of a dishonoured cheque. The plaintiff claimed that the cheque was issued as the price of goods supplied. The defendant admitted having issued the cheque but denied any privity of contract with the plaintiff and offered an alternative explanation for the cheque's issuance. The trial court, having considered the affidavits of both parties, granted unconditional leave to defend. The Delhi High Court, in revision under Section 115 of the Code, interfered with this order on the ground that the defence was not bona fide and imposed a condition of deposit. The Supreme Court set aside the High Court's order and restored the unconditional leave granted by the trial court.

In doing so, the Supreme Court in Mechelec adopted and approved the fivefold framework originally stated by Das J. in Smt. Kiranmoyee Dassi v. Dr. J. Chatterjee (1949) 53 CWN 246 and applicable to Order XXXVII proceedings. The framework, as applied by the Supreme Court, may be stated as follows. a) If the defendant satisfies the court that the defence is good on its merits, unconditional leave must be granted. b) If the defendant raises a triable issue indicating a fair or bona fide or reasonable defence, even if not a positively good one, unconditional leave must be granted. c) If the defence is not bona fide but the defendant raises a plausible case that calls for investigation, the court may grant conditional leave, typically requiring the defendant to deposit a specified sum as a condition of being allowed to defend. d) If the defence is illusory, a sham, or practically moonshine, the court may protect the plaintiff by granting only conditional leave requiring payment into court or the furnishing of security. e) If the defendant has no defence or the defence is manifestly dishonest, no leave should be granted at all and the plaintiff is entitled to judgment.

The Mechelec framework draws a crucial distinction between a defence that is dishonest or entirely without foundation, which justifies refusal or conditional leave, and a defence that is merely doubtful or unlikely to succeed at trial, which nonetheless entitles the defendant to unconditional leave. The Supreme Court emphasised that a trial court cannot reach a conclusion that a defence is not bona fide arbitrarily, and that conditional leave is not a tool for punishing defendants whose cases appear weak. The exercise of the discretion to impose conditions must itself be principled and proportionate. This aspect of the decision has important consequences for the degree to which a trial court may inquire into the merits at the leave stage: the inquiry is necessarily preliminary, and the court must be careful not to pre-empt a trial on the merits by treating a contested defence as conclusively dishonest without the benefit of evidence and cross-examination.

V. Conditional Leave, Post-Grant Procedure, and Default

Where conditional leave is granted, typically in the form of a direction to deposit a sum in court within a specified time, the question arises as to the consequences of non-compliance. Order XXXVII Rule 3(5) provides that if the conditions on which leave was granted are not complied with by the defendant, the plaintiff becomes entitled to judgment forthwith. The courts have consistently held that this entitlement follows automatically from the default in complying with the condition, without requiring any further inquiry into the merits. The Jammu and Kashmir and Ladakh High Court reiterated this position in a 2025 decision, holding that a court adjudicating a summary suit is within its powers to pass a decree forthwith upon the defendant's failure to comply with the conditions imposed when leave was granted.

Once leave to defend is unconditionally granted, or granted on terms that are duly complied with, the suit proceeds as an ordinary suit in the court from the stage of filing of the written statement. The defendant files a written statement, issues are framed, and the matter proceeds to trial. The advantage to the plaintiff at this stage lies principally in the procedural streamlining that has already occurred: issues of maintainability, the existence of the instrument, and the prima facie liability have effectively been filtered at the leave stage, narrowing the scope of the trial to the specific defence pleaded in the affidavit and the written statement.

An important ancillary question is whether a court may, at the leave stage, pass a decree for such part of the amount as the defendant admits to be due, while granting leave to contest the remainder. Order XXXVII Rule 3(5) expressly provides for this scenario, authorising the court to pass judgment in favour of the plaintiff for the admitted portion. This provision prevents defendants from using a partial or technical defence to delay execution on the entirety of the claim, and reflects the legislative intent to confine the summary procedure to genuine disputes rather than instruments of delay.

VI. Setting Aside Decrees under Rule 4: The "Special Circumstances" Requirement

Order XXXVII Rule 4 empowers the court that has passed an ex parte decree in a summary suit to set aside the decree, stay or set aside its execution, or grant leave to the defendant to appear and defend the suit, provided that "special circumstances" are shown. The expression "special circumstances" is not defined in the Code. As the Supreme Court explained in the context of this Rule, the phrase connotes something exceptional, extraordinary, or uncommon, and must be construed in light of the individual facts of each case rather than through the application of a rigid formula.

Non-service of the summons is the clearest and most uncontroversial instance of special circumstances. Where the defendant can demonstrate that the summons were never served, whether by reason of incorrect address, improper mode of service, or administrative error, the court is expected to set aside the decree and afford the defendant an opportunity to defend. The mere statement by the defendant that service was not effected is, however, insufficient; the defendant must place materials before the court to substantiate the claim of non-service. Beyond non-service, courts have found special circumstances in cases of fraud by the plaintiff in the institution of the suit, sudden illness or incapacitation of the defendant at the critical procedural stage, and, in limited cases, genuine ignorance of the proceedings attributable to circumstances beyond the defendant's control.

The Supreme Court in Mahesh Kumar Joshi v. Madan Singh Negi (2023) held that an application under Order XXXVII Rule 4 cannot be entertained in routine, and that the court must balance the equities between the parties when deciding whether to set aside a decree, appropriate conditions may be imposed if the defendant makes out a debatable case that would prima facie indicate injustice if the ex parte decree were to stand. This approach confirms that Rule 4 is not a general door for setting aside summary decrees; it is a limited safety valve for genuinely exceptional situations, and its operation must be consistent with the primary purpose of the summary procedure, which is the expeditious resolution of monetary claims grounded in acknowledged written obligations.

VII. Order XXXVII and the Commercial Courts Act, 2015: A Comparative Overview

The Commercial Courts Act, 2015, as amended in 2018, introduced a parallel summary mechanism in Order XIII-A of the Code for commercial disputes of a specified value, currently set at three lakh rupees. Under Order XIII-A, either party to a pending commercial suit may apply for summary judgment on the ground that the other party has no real prospect of succeeding on the claim or the defence, as the case may be, and that there is no other compelling reason why the matter should proceed to a full trial. The application may be filed at any time after service of summons but before the framing of issues.

Order XXXVII and Order XIII-A operate differently in concept and procedure. Under Order XXXVII, the plaintiff elects the summary procedure at the time of institution and the suit is structured as a summary suit from the outset; the defendant must seek leave to defend, and the burden of establishing a triable issue lies on the defendant. Under Order XIII-A, any party may apply for summary judgment in an existing commercial suit, and the burden lies on the applicant to demonstrate that there is no real prospect of the opposing party succeeding, a standard drawn in design from Order 24 of the English Civil Procedure Rules 1998. The Delhi High Court has clarified, in the context of the Manish Kumar Goel decision of 2023, that a summary suit filed under Order XXXVII before a Commercial Court is not subject to the summary judgment procedure under Order XIII-A, the two regimes being mutually exclusive in their application. An application for summary judgment under Order XIII-A is expressly not available in a suit originally filed as a summary suit under Order XXXVII.

The practical significance of this distinction is considerable. A plaintiff with a strong documentary case based on a written contract or negotiable instrument can elect between filing a summary suit under Order XXXVII and seeking summary judgment under Order XIII-A in an ordinary commercial suit. The Order XXXVII route offers the advantage of placing the burden on the defendant from the outset and producing a decree without trial in the event of non-appearance or failure to obtain leave. The Order XIII-A route, by contrast, may be pursued in suits where the claim is complex or involves non-monetary relief not cognisable under Order XXXVII, and offers the possibility of disposing of even non-summary suits without a full trial on evidence. The two mechanisms together represent a significant expansion of the courts' capacity to prevent the procedural process from being deployed as an instrument of delay in straightforward commercial matters.

VIII. Conclusion

Order XXXVII of the Code of Civil Procedure embodies a considered legislative judgment that certain classes of money claims, grounded in written instruments and negotiable instruments, are not appropriate candidates for the full machinery of a civil trial unless the defendant can demonstrate, at the threshold, that there is something genuinely worth trying. The summary suit is not, as is sometimes mischaracterised, a device that bypasses the defendant's rights; it is a device that tests those rights early, filters out fictitious defences, and reserves trial for disputes that warrant it.

The doctrinal framework for leave to defend, as articulated in Mechelec Engineers and Manufacturers v. Basic Equipment Corporation (1976), remains coherent and workable. Its central virtue is the preservation of judicial discretion calibrated to the facts: courts are not required to refuse leave wherever a defence appears unlikely to succeed, but they are not obliged to permit the summary procedure to be defeated by a defence that is transparently without substance. The gradation of unconditional leave, conditional leave, and outright refusal maps onto the spectrum of cases that arise in practice and gives courts an adequate range of responses.

The intersection with the Commercial Courts Act, 2015 has added a dimension that practitioners must navigate with care. The availability of Order XIII-A summary judgment in commercial suits that fall outside the ambit of Order XXXVII, and the exclusion of Order XIII-A in suits originally instituted as summary suits, means that the choice of procedure at the point of institution has consequences that go beyond mere timing. As the volume of commercial litigation before the designated commercial courts in Bengaluru and other centres grows, these procedural choices are likely to acquire increasing significance in shaping the pace and character of commercial dispute resolution.
